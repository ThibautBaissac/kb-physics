# Cosmic Paradox Reveals the Awful Consequence of an Observer-Free Universe

## Introduction

Theoretical physicists working at the intersection of quantum mechanics and gravity have encountered a perplexing mathematical result. When researchers examined a closed universe using quantum gravity calculations in 2019, they discovered something startling: the universe appeared to exist in only a single quantum state, containing no measurable information — not even a single bit of data.

This finding contradicts observable reality. As physicist Rob Myers from the Perimeter Institute notes, "We look around, and certainly the world seems more complex than that."

## A Shocking Argument

The breakthrough came through holography, a concept stemming from Juan Maldacena's landmark 1995 work. Maldacena demonstrated that string theory calculations could be simplified using the "anti-de Sitter" geometry — a universe with boundaries resembling a tin can. Within this framework, everything occurring inside the can appears as shadows on its outer boundary, like a 3D projection onto a flat screen.

Building on this foundation, Maldacena and colleagues at the Institute for Advanced Study developed the "island formula" in 2019, which tracks regions within black holes. This tool helped explain how black holes can release information despite their gravitational barriers.

However, when Maldacena applied the island formula to closed universes — cosmic structures with no edges or boundaries — he uncovered disturbing implications. Ying Zhao, one of his collaborators, recalled being "pretty shocked by that argument" and initially attempted to refute it.

## Blank Slate

Physicists describe quantum systems using Hilbert spaces — abstract mathematical spaces where additional dimensions represent additional information. A simple system like a computer bit has two dimensions. Most quantum systems, including hydrogen atoms, possess infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces reflecting their unlimited possible states.

Yet Maldacena's calculations revealed that closed universes possess one-dimensional Hilbert spaces — meaning they contain only one possible quantum state. Edgar Shaghoulian, a physicist at UC Santa Cruz, found this paradoxical: "On my desk there are an infinite number of states."

The pattern persisted across different theoretical models. Physicist Henry Maxfield and collaborator Donald Marolf examined hypothetical spacetime bubbles called baby universes and found identical simplicity. The apparent emptiness became increasingly difficult to dismiss.

## Complexity Returns

In 2023, Shaghoulian noted that topological field theories — mathematical frameworks used to study geometric shapes — exhibited similar single-dimensional Hilbert spaces. However, when subdividing these spaces into zones, the Hilbert space enlarged to accommodate multiple descriptions. "The rules of the game change," he explained.

Shaghoulian proposed introducing an observer into closed universes, analogous to how subdivisions enlarge Hilbert spaces in topological field theories.

Quantum mechanics inherently distinguishes between observers (conducting experiments) and observed systems (typically small quantum entities). The observer exists in the classical realm, far from quantum effects. Shaghoulian hypothesized this division might similarly expand the Hilbert spaces of closed universes.

In 2024, Ying Zhao moved to MIT and began developing this concept with colleagues Daniel Harlow and Mykhaylo Usatyuk. They conceptualized the observer as introducing a boundary — not around the universe itself, but around the observer. Their calculations demonstrated that when a classical observer inhabits a closed universe, the apparent complexity returns.

The MIT team published their findings in January 2025, with another group simultaneously presenting similar ideas. Additional researchers noted connections to earlier work.

## Current Status

Physicists emphasize they haven't found the complete solution. The paradox itself might represent a misunderstanding that dissolves under closer scrutiny. However, incorporating observers into closed universe models appears promising.

Zhao cautiously stated: "Am I really confident to say that it's right, it's _the_ thing that solves the problem? I cannot say that. We try our best."

## Paradigm Shift

If this framework proves correct, it would represent fundamental change in physics. Traditionally, physicists pursue an objective "view from nowhere" — a description of nature independent of observers. But understanding closed universes through observer-dependent boundaries suggests such observer-independent descriptions may be impossible.

This shift implies that subjective perspectives, views from specific locations by specific observers, might constitute all that physics can ever achieve. The traditional goal of understanding how observers emerge within an objective universe would invert: observers themselves might be essential to defining universal complexity.
