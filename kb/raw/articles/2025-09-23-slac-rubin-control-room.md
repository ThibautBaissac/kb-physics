# Remote eyes on the sky: Inside SLAC's Rubin control room

With survey operations beginning this fall, the Vera C. Rubin Observatory control room at SLAC serves as a crucial hub for training and remote observing support for the NSF–DOE facility.

In April 2025, scientists and engineers gathered at SLAC's Rubin Operations Center in Menlo Park, California to witness a historic moment: "First Photon," the initial raw image captured by the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) Camera. The image streamed live from Cerro Pachón in northern Chile, where the telescope is located thousands of kilometers away.

The control room displays real-time technical data across six massive monitors, showing camera diagnostics, telescope orientation, temperature readings, and weather conditions. A live video feed maintains constant contact with the main control room at the observatory.

As operating partner, SLAC oversees the LSST Camera's stewardship, ensuring smooth operation and data management. The facility is equipped with identical software and supervision tools as the summit's main control room, positioning it advantageously near the camera's engineers and future data scientists.

Phil Marshall, SLAC's senior scientist and deputy director for Rubin Operations, explained: "It's like having a team of detectives...incredibly helpful to have someone back at headquarters, on the radio, helping them put the pieces together."

Shuang Liang, a postdoctoral researcher at SLAC, assembled and commissioned the control room. He trained using the LSST Operations and Visualization Environment (LOVE), mastering skills for monitoring instruments and assessing observation quality. Following training, Liang spent two years stationed in Chile as an observing specialist, rotating between daytime and night observation shifts.

During nighttime observations, Liang describes monitoring telescope focus and making critical decisions about weather conditions. He notes that commissioning scientists and engineers—both on-site and remote—provide expert troubleshooting support when anomalies occur.

Marshall emphasizes that hands-on operational experience provides postdocs deep understanding of data collection processes and low-level data issues. This model mirrors large-scale particle physics projects like CERN's Large Hadron Collider, where postdocs work directly on detectors before analyzing data.

After completing their two-year Chilean tenure, postdocs return to SLAC for another two years, dedicating 80% to research and 20% to operations support, including remote observing assistance from California.
