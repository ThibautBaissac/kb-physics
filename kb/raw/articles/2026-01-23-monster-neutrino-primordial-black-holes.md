# Monster Neutrino Could Be a Messenger of Ancient Black Holes

Nearly three years ago, a particle from space struck the Mediterranean Sea and activated the partially complete Cubic Kilometer Neutrino Telescope (KM3NET) detector off Sicily's coast. This neutrino was extraordinary—35 times more energetic than any previously detected neutrino, according to measurements from comparable observatories like IceCube in Antarctica.

Scientists initially attributed the particle to highly active galaxies called blazars or background cosmogenic sources. However, physicist David Kaiser from MIT proposed an intriguing alternative: the neutrino might have originated from an exploding primordial black hole.

## The Primordial Black Hole Concept

Primordial black holes (PBHs) are theoretical objects that could have formed during the universe's first moments. The concept emerged from work by Soviet physicists Yakov Zel'dovich and Igor Novikov in 1966, later developed by Stephen Hawking and Bernard Carr in the 1970s.

According to theory, density spikes during cosmic expansion could have collapsed into black holes of varying masses. Some could have been atomic-nucleus-sized. "They could form before there were even atoms, let alone stars," Kaiser explained.

Hawking radiation represents a critical aspect of PBH behavior. Black holes lose mass by emitting particles through quantum effects at their edges. A primordial black hole originally nuclear-sized would eventually evaporate. "Very little mass gets radiated over the majority of the black hole's lifetime," noted Alexandra Klipfel, a doctoral student working with Kaiser. "But then, right at the end, it emits a majority of its mass in a very rapid explosion."

The final burst would release all 17 fundamental particles in the Standard Model. According to Kaiser, this explosion would produce "neutrinos and quarks and all kinds of exotic things."

## Mass Windows and Dark Matter

Scientists have constrained possible PBH masses. Too-small black holes would have evaporated already. Too-large ones would have created observable gravitational effects on distant starlight. The viable range spans from approximately 100 quadrillion grams (asteroid mass) to 100 sextillion grams (lunar mass). Smaller PBHs around 100 trillion grams would be finishing their evaporation today.

If PBHs exist within these windows, they could constitute dark matter—the invisible substance comprising most of the universe's mass. "They're one of the few good theories for what dark matter could be," explained Wenzer Qin, a theoretical physicist at New York University.

However, the concept faces challenges. Incorporating PBHs into early-universe models requires extremely precise parameter adjustments. "You have to tune the knob just right," Qin stated.

## The Mathematical Case

Kaiser and Klipfel published their analysis in *Physical Review Letters* on September 18, 2025. Their calculations showed that a primordial black hole with initial asteroid mass, exploding approximately 2,000 astronomical units away (2,000 times Earth-Sun distance), could have produced the detected neutrino.

"We found about an 8% chance of this happening," Klipfel said. "It's a low-probability event. But it's not a completely impossible event."

The PBH would have steadily emitted Hawking radiation throughout the universe's 13.8-billion-year history before its final explosive moment. The distance was critical—closer proximity would have produced detectable gamma rays; greater distance would have dispersed particles too broadly.

## Scientific Skepticism

Not all researchers support this hypothesis. Dan Hooper, a cosmologist at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, remains unconvinced: "I would bet an awful lot of money that it has nothing to do with primordial black holes," he stated, adding that exploding black holes would be easily detectable if they existed.

Other scientists express cautious interest. Evan McDonough from the University of Winnipeg suggested "there is probably at least one PBH out there," though he questioned whether they constitute significant dark matter quantities. Priyamvada Natarajan from Yale University argued that while PBHs likely represent only tiny dark matter fractions, they force scientists to reconsider alternatives beyond WIMPs (weakly interacting massive particles) and axions.

## Future Detection Methods

Multiple approaches could reveal PBH evidence. Gravitational-wave detectors might identify mergers of PBHs smaller than stellar mass. Andrea Thamm and colleagues proposed that exotic particles—dark photons and dark electrons—could slow Hawking radiation emission, potentially meaning more PBHs are evaporating today. They suggest a "more than 90% chance" of spotting such evaporation within ten years.

Kaiser himself proposes using spacecraft orbiting Mars to detect gravitational wobbles from nearby PBHs. "At any given time, there would be at least one PBH in the solar system," he suggested, estimating that every three to ten years, one could approach Mars closely enough to create "tiny but measurable" orbital changes measurable in tens of centimeters.

## Conclusion

Bernard Carr, who worked with Hawking on PBH theory fifty years ago, finds the KM3NET detection intriguing. "This could be evidence for black hole explosions," he remarked, acknowledging decades of seeking confirmation without previous evidence.

Kaiser concluded optimistically about the coincidence: "The numbers are outrageously congruent, considering I walked in half-joking."

The question remains open: did scientists just observe a messenger from an ancient, exploding primordial black hole?
