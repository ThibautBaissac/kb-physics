# Elusive romance of top-quark pairs observed at the LHC

The CMS and ATLAS experiments at CERN's Large Hadron Collider have observed an unexpected behavior in top quarks, suggesting these heaviest elementary particles briefly form paired structures. This finding, presented at the European Physical Society's High-Energy Physics conference in Marseille, indicates top quarks can momentarily unite with antimatter counterparts to produce a "quasi-bound-state" called toponium.

Routine proton-proton collisions at the LHC generate top quark-antiquark pairs. Measuring this process's probability serves as both a Standard Model test and a tool for detecting unknown particles.

CMS researchers, analyzing 2016-2018 data while searching for Higgs boson variants, discovered an unexpected surplus of top quark-antiquark pairs appearing at minimum production energy. They considered the hypothesis of a short-lived union—something previously deemed undetectable at the LHC.

Top quarks typically decay almost instantly, preventing bound state formation that other quarks achieve. However, quantum mechanics permits top quark-antiquark pairs produced nearly at rest to occasionally exchange gluons, binding into toponium.

CMS measured the cross section at "8.8 picobarns with an uncertainty of about 1.3 picobarns," achieving five-sigma certainty—the discovery threshold in particle physics.

ATLAS confirmed this phenomenon across their full Run-2 dataset (2015-2018), rejecting models ignoring quasi-bound-state formation "with a significance of 7.7 sigma" and determining the cross section as "9.0± 1.3 picobarns," closely matching CMS results.

While the phenomenon is confirmed, its underlying cause remains uncertain. Possibilities include toponium formation or an undiscovered particle with mass near twice the top quark's mass. Resolution requires advanced quantum chromodynamics calculations modeling quark-gluon behavior in high-energy collisions.

This discovery would expand the quarkonium story. Charmonium was discovered in 1974, triggering the "November Revolution," while bottomonium followed three years later, both in U.S. laboratories.

Run 3 of the LHC, delivering substantially more data, will enable deeper exploration of the strong force through top quark interactions in the non-relativistic regime.
