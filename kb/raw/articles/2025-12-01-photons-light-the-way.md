# Photons light the way

Photons, the fundamental particles of the electromagnetic field that permeate the universe, don't have mass. They don't have charge. Because they travel at the cosmic speed limit, they don't experience time.

They sound completely alien. And yet photons are the quanta of light that allow us to observe the world around us—both up close and from afar.

Distinguished professor emeritus Claudio Pellegrini from UCLA notes that "photons have always been key to how we observe and comprehend nature."

## Understanding and harnessing light

In the 1600s, Galileo Galilei used telescopes and microscopes to examine the heavens and the infinitesimal, discovering celestial features that challenged geocentric models of the cosmos.

That same century, scientists began investigating light's nature. In 1675, Isaac Newton proposed light consisted of tiny particles called corpuscles. However, this raised a problem: if space were empty between particles, how could light traverse from moon to Earth?

By the nineteenth century, scientists favored the ether theory—a universal medium enabling light propagation. Physicist James Clerk Maxwell demonstrated that photon wavelengths determined their electromagnetic spectrum position, ranging from radio waves to gamma rays.

Max Planck later theorized light behaved as both particle and wave. Albert Einstein disagreed, proposing in 1905 that light comprised discrete energy packets. Arthur Compton's experiments demonstrated photons exhibited both particle and wave characteristics, leading to acceptance of wave-particle duality once Louis de Broglie extended this concept to other particles.

## Photons today

Contemporary understanding of wave-particle duality enables scientists to conceptualize photons flexibly during experiments, fostering technological innovations in lasers, microscopes, and quantum computers.

The James Webb Space Telescope now enables observations stretching back over 13.5 billion years using infrared light collection.

SLAC's Stanford Synchrotron Radiation Lightsource has employed intense X-ray beams for thirty-five years to image cellular and solar structures at molecular resolution. The Linac Coherent Light Source extends this capability, permitting researchers to observe individual atomic motion during chemical processes.

Uwe Bergmann from University of Wisconsin-Madison has utilized SLAC's light sources for humanities research, revealing ancient texts. He recovered Archimedes' mathematical writings from a twelfth-century parchment palimpsest by detecting iron in original ink through X-rays.

Photons enable quantum technology development through polarization states and quantum entanglement—phenomena allowing particles to share quantum states. Scientists demonstrated photon entanglement possibilities from the 1970s through 1990s, catalyzing the quantum information science revolution.

Pellegrini observes that "photons have demonstrated quantum mechanics's strangest characteristics," with recent collaborations at LCLS exploiting these properties.

As researchers enhance instruments for examining cosmic and subatomic scales, photons will remain essential to scientific discovery.
