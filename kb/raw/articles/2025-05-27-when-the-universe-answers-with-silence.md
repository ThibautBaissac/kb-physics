# When the universe answers with silence

*By Maxwell Bernstein, Symmetry Magazine, May 27, 2025*

For centuries, scientists believed the universe contained a mysterious substance called the luminiferous ether that transmitted light waves through empty space. In 1887, physicists Albert A. Michelson and Edward W. Morley tested this hypothesis by measuring the speed of light in various directions. Their groundbreaking experiment produced a null result — no variation in light speed — challenging the ether hypothesis and forcing the physics community to reconsider fundamental assumptions about the nature of light and space.

Today's particle physicists face a similar moment. As Flip Tanedo notes, "at the time, it was obvious to everybody that they would detect the ether." Matthew McCullough observes that disproving favored theories pushes scientists "back to the drawing board in a way that winds up deepening our understanding" of physical reality.

## The SUSY Problem

Modern physics now encounters more null results than discoveries. The Standard Model, developed in the 1960s–70s, successfully explained fundamental particles and forces. Extensions like supersymmetry (SUSY) promised elegant solutions to remaining mysteries: SUSY proposed that every known particle has a heavier "superpartner," which would solve the hierarchy problem (why the Higgs mass is so small compared to the Planck scale) and provide natural dark matter candidates.

When the Large Hadron Collider began operations in 2010, physicists widely anticipated discovering SUSY particles alongside the Higgs boson, which itself was expected and quickly found in 2012. But SUSY remained elusive. Maurizio Pierini reflected: "it's like we set out to win a 100-meter race only to find we were running a marathon."

## The Higgs Mystery

The Higgs mass of 125 GeV sits precisely where the Standard Model should break down — what Pierini calls "the smoking gun of new physics." The hierarchy problem predicts that quantum corrections should drive the Higgs mass to much higher values unless new physics intervenes at this energy scale. Yet despite searches in the surrounding energy ranges, LHC experiments have yielded no new particles. As Pierini put it, there is "only smoke."

## Moving Forward

Rather than abandoning theoretical ambitions, physicists are now investigating alternative possibilities:

- **Long-lived particles:** Some SUSY scenarios predict particles with unusual detector signatures — particles that travel significant distances before decaying, leaving tracks in outer detector regions rather than the central vertex. These had not been priority search targets in the LHC's early years.
- **Ultralight axions:** Dark matter candidates that are far lighter than WIMPs, detectable with specialized low-energy experiments rather than high-energy colliders.
- **High-Luminosity LHC:** The HL-LHC upgrade will increase the dataset tenfold, dramatically improving sensitivity to rare processes and subtle deviations from Standard Model predictions.

The historical parallel is instructive: the Michelson-Morley null result did not end physics — it prompted Einstein's theory of special relativity, one of the most consequential advances in science. McCullough and others hold that the current null results, frustrating as they are, may be similarly generative: "Disproving favored theories pushes scientists back to the drawing board in a way that winds up deepening our understanding."
