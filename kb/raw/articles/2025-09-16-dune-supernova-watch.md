# DUNE experiment prepares for supernova watch

The Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE) is preparing to serve a dual purpose: studying neutrinos while simultaneously acting as an early warning system for supernovae in our galaxy.

## The Science Behind Supernovae

When massive stars — at least eight times the sun's mass — reach the end of their lives, they explode in core-collapse supernovae. Though visually spectacular, these events release approximately 99% of their energy as neutrinos and antineutrinos rather than light. Because neutrinos rarely interact with matter, they escape the collapsing stellar core rapidly, arriving on Earth before the visible light. This makes them valuable as advance notice for astronomers worldwide.

## DUNE's Role

Located at the Sanford Underground Research Facility in South Dakota, DUNE's detectors will continuously collect neutrino data. Researchers have developed "trigger" algorithms to quickly identify supernova signals among millions of recorded interactions. Upon detection, alerts will be sent globally, giving astronomers time to orient telescopes toward the explosion's location.

## Unique Capabilities

Unlike previous experiments detecting only antineutrinos, DUNE can detect both neutrinos and antineutrinos, enabling comprehensive matter-antimatter comparisons. Scientists emphasize that neutrinos function as "black-box recorders" revealing stellar interior physics under conditions impossible to recreate elsewhere on Earth.
