# Dark Matter: A Worldwide Search

Something is out there. As far as scientists know, just 15% of the matter in the universe is the ordinary kind we can see. The other 85%, called dark matter, remains beyond detection, invisibly affecting the movements of galaxies and subtly warping our view of the stars.

Something is out there, but it's also right here. As far as scientists know, dark matter passes through us as we also pass through it on Earth's journey through the cosmos.

But what is it? Does it act like a particle? Does it act like a wave? Is there an entire hidden ecosystem of dark particles, mirroring the different types of matter we can see?

Scientists are using a variety of methods to try to find out. Some look for dark matter through indirect detection experiments, which look for the energy released during dark matter's interactions with other particles, on Earth or in space. Others look for dark matter through direct detection experiments, which wait for dark matter to interact with the experiment itself. Still others look for dark matter through accelerator-based experiments, which attempt to coax particles of dark matter from the energy in particle collisions. Connecting astronomy and the fundamental properties of dark matter are searches for dark matter self-interactions, which examine the density of dark matter in galaxies.

Scientists from around the world collaborate on projects located across the globe and share the data they collect. Working together, we can gain a better understanding of the universe we all share.
