# Astrophysicists Find No 'Hair' on Black Holes

**Author:** Matt von Hippel  
**Published:** August 27, 2025 (updated September 3, 2025)  
**Publication:** Quanta Magazine

## What Einstein Didn't Predict

Einstein's general relativity predicts that black holes are extraordinarily simple objects: characterized entirely by their mass and rotation speed. This is the "no-hair conjecture" — all black holes with the same mass and spin should behave identically, regardless of the material that formed them or their formation history. The name comes from the saying "black holes have no hair," meaning no additional distinguishing features.

But this simplicity creates a problem. When matter falls into a black hole, general relativity says all information about that matter — its quantum state, composition, history — is erased except for mass and spin. Quantum mechanics, however, requires that information be preserved. This conflict is the black hole information paradox.

To resolve the paradox, various alternatives to smooth, featureless event horizons have been proposed. These would give black holes subtle "hair" — distinguishing features that encode the information of infalling matter:

- **Firewalls**: A shell of extremely high-energy particles just outside the event horizon, proposed in a 2012 thought experiment connecting the information paradox to horizon structure. An astronaut approaching such a horizon would be incinerated rather than passing through smoothly.
- **Fuzzballs**: Proposed by Samir Mathur. Rather than a classical spacetime, a black hole would be a quantum superposition of multiple spacetime configurations, creating fuzzy edges instead of a sharp horizon.
- **Gravastars**: Theoretical objects resembling black holes from the outside but surrounded by shells of exotic matter.
- **Regular black holes**: Reimagined black holes without central singularities — removing the infinitely dense points that plague general relativity.

## Short Hair, Long Hair

The key question is not merely whether black holes have "hair" but where it would reside. Hair arising from quantum gravity — like the Planck length (10⁻³³ centimeters) — would be invisible to any conceivable detector. Hair at the scale of the horizon itself, however, would be detectable through gravitational waves from black hole mergers.

Vitor Cardoso (Niels Bohr Institute, Copenhagen): "As years went by and the events piled up, we realized that we could have stronger, more robust tests of the theory [of general relativity] — or alternatives."

The LIGO collaboration first detected gravitational waves from colliding black holes in 2015. Over the following decade, hundreds of signals have been recorded. Each collision produces a characteristic "ringdown" — gravitational wave oscillations as the merged black hole settles into its final state. This ringdown is exquisitely sensitive to the structure of the black hole's exterior.

## Math Meets Data

Vitor Cardoso and colleagues — including Gregorio Carullo (University of Birmingham, formerly Copenhagen) and Simon Maenaut (graduate student, KU Leuven) — compiled data from 22 black hole collision events. Carullo and Maenaut: "We sort of jumped on a free desk and started coding together."

A key technical breakthrough came in 2023 from KU Leuven (arxiv.org/abs/2304.02663): the team developed a technique for calculating the behavior of fast-spinning black holes under modified versions of Einstein's theory. Previously, such calculations could only be done for slowly rotating black holes — a severe limitation since real black holes spin rapidly.

The full analysis (arxiv.org/abs/2411.17893, released May 2025) combined the 22 events to constrain deviations from general relativity. Result: **any deviation from Einstein's predictions must be confined to within 40 kilometers of the event horizon**, at 95% confidence. A comprehensive review of multiple testing methods is available at arxiv.org/abs/2505.23895.

Niayesh Afshordi (University of Waterloo): "I think it's a worthwhile exercise to go and test that."

## On the Horizon

The 40 km constraint is impressive, but horizon-scale quantum effects for stellar-mass black holes would appear at scales far smaller than 40 km. Next-generation gravitational wave detectors will dramatically improve this:

- **Einstein Telescope** (Europe): Will observe all black holes in the universe within a certain mass range. Precision sufficient to detect deviations at the scale of a football field's length. Carullo: "The Einstein Telescope will basically observe all the black holes in the universe of a certain mass range … which is incredible."
- **Cosmic Explorer** (United States): Similarly enhanced sensitivity.
- **Indian LIGO detector**: Expected around 2030.

Current detectors: LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory, first detection 2015), Virgo (Europe, first signal 2017), KAGRA (Japan).

Roy Kerr calculated the behavior of rotating black holes in 1963 (the Kerr metric), providing the mathematical framework that all these tests rely on.

Simon Maenaut: "It's possible that we will confirm Einstein's theory of general relativity to five decimal places, and that would be great, but it's also possible that we come across something that we didn't expect."
