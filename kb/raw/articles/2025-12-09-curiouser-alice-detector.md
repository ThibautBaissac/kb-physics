# Curiouser and curiouser: a riddle at the ALICE detector

*By Sarah Charley | Symmetry Magazine | December 9, 2025*

In 2023, the ALICE experiment at CERN faced a mysterious crisis during their crucial heavy-ion run. Sarah Porteboeuf, serving as the ALICE Run Coordinator, had anticipated an excellent data-collection period. The detector had undergone major upgrades between 2019 and 2021, and the collaboration hadn't collected new lead-ion data since 2018 due to planned maintenance and an unplanned helium leak in 2022.

When collisions began, Porteboeuf immediately noticed something troubling. "During the first two minutes of data taking, I saw that our inner tracking system had a problem: There was a hole," she explained. Rather than the expected symmetrical fireworks pattern of particle tracks, a significant portion of the detector remained dark.

The team projected the malfunction would impact approximately one-quarter of the entire experiment. Initial investigations ruled out detector misconfiguration. Instead, evidence pointed to the LHC beam itself — specifically, stray particles somehow traveling off-course and overwhelming the tracker through the collimator protection system upstream of ALICE.

Accelerator physicist Roderik Bruce led investigations into the beam anomaly. Simulations revealed a striking possibility: the beam may have become contaminated with lead-207 ions, which possess half a percent less mass than the intended lead-208 ions. This mass difference would cause the lighter ions to bend excessively through the LHC's magnetic system, creating an oscillating secondary beam.

Testing confirmed the hypothesis. The contamination originated from collimators at a location called IR7, approximately 10 kilometers upstream, where the LHC's "trash bin" operations occasionally stripped neutrons from lead-208 ions.

The solution involved deliberately bumping the unwanted lead-207 beam out of phase with the main beam, causing it to bypass the ALICE collimator entirely and collide with distant collimators. This intervention reduced background signals by a factor of 100.

Though the first 10 days of data-taking were lost to troubleshooting, ALICE ultimately collected more data during 2023 than from all preceding lead-ion runs combined. "This was an excellent year," Porteboeuf reflected, emphasizing the collaborative spirit that enabled the rapid solution.
