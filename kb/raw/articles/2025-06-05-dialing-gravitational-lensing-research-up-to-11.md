# Dialing gravitational lensing research up to 11

The NSF–DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory will soon dramatically expand gravitational lensing research through its Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST).

## The Science of Gravitational Lensing

Light traveling from distant stars bends when passing through warped space around massive objects. Astrophysicists study these distortions in distant galaxy shapes to map invisible dark matter. As Theo Schutt, a Stanford University graduate student, explains: "We can use the gravitational lensing effect to measure the invisible dark matter by observing how distant galaxy images become distorted by intervening matter."

The field involves two primary types: **weak lensing**, where galaxy shapes undergo mild, coherent changes, and **strong lensing**, where a background galaxy aligns directly behind a massive foreground object, creating multiple viewable images of the same distant source.

Deeper combined images improve understanding of invisible mass warping distant light. However, telescope power and analysis speed currently limit research scope.

## LSST's Revolutionary Capacity

The Rubin Observatory's 3,200-megapixel LSST Camera will collect over 20 terabytes of data nightly across a decade — vastly exceeding previous cosmological datasets. This unprecedented data volume will transform gravitational lensing studies:

- Known **strong gravitational lenses** may increase from roughly 1,000 to **tens of thousands**
- **Weak lensing** examples could jump from hundreds of millions to **billions**
- **Lensed supernovae** detections will increase by at least 100-fold, reaching approximately 50 annually

Simon Birrer notes that "Rubin will be a game changer" for lensed supernovae research. Integration with complementary surveys like DESI and Euclid will further enhance analysis.

## Preparation and Collaboration

Scientists are conducting simulations and open data challenges to prepare analysis pipelines before LSST begins full operations. The research community emphasizes that collaborative software development will be essential for managing the incoming data volume. As Rachel Mandelbaum states: "We can do better science together."

Community collaboration and shared software development will prove essential for handling the unprecedented scale of Rubin's gravitational lensing datasets.
