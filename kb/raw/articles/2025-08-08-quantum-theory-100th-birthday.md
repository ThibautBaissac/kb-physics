# 'It's a Mess': A Brain-Bending Trip to Quantum Theory's 100th Birthday Party

*By Charlie Wood, Quanta Magazine, August 8, 2025*

In June 2025, approximately 300 physicists gathered in Hamburg before traveling to Helgoland in the North Sea to celebrate the centennial of quantum mechanics. The conference marked 100 years since Werner Heisenberg's breakthrough calculations on this windswept German island in 1925.

## Heisenberg's Revolutionary Insight

Heisenberg's contribution fundamentally reshaped physics. Rather than attempting to visualize atomic structures directly — an impossible task given experimental limitations — he focused exclusively on measurable quantities: the color and intensity of emitted light. This observation-based framework led to the mathematical matrices that form quantum mechanics' foundation.

One physicist noted: "I feel like I'm at Woodstock. It's my only chance to see them all in one place."

As the conference boat departed Hamburg, tensions emerged immediately. One researcher exclaimed: "We are here happily celebrating 100 years, but actually it's a mess. We were given this theory, and we still don't understand what it means."

## The Measurement Problem

Despite quantum mechanics' spectacular technological success — atomic clocks, quantum computers, cryptography — fundamental questions persist about what the theory actually describes.

The theory predicts probabilities rather than definite outcomes. When measured, a quantum state (represented by ψ) "collapses" to a single result. Yet physicists remain divided about whether ψ represents physical reality or merely our incomplete knowledge.

A prominent physicist stated: "It's just embarrassing that we don't have a story we can tell people about what reality is."

## Competing Interpretations

The conference revealed deep disagreements among physicists about quantum mechanics' meaning:

**Copenhagen Interpretation:** The traditional view holds that ψ describes knowledge rather than reality itself. One Nobel laureate defended this approach: "We can only rely on our knowledge. It's the only thing we have."

**QBism:** Chris Fuchs champions an interpretation where quantum mechanics serves as "a user's manual for the world." According to this view: "A quantum state is a catalog of my degrees of belief about the consequences of my actions."

**Many-Worlds:** This interpretation suggests all possibilities in ψ are equally real, with reality constantly splitting into parallel branches.

**Relational Mechanics:** Carlo Rovelli proposes that properties exist only relative to interactions between objects.

One researcher summarized the fragmentation: "I do not understand quantum mechanics. I don't know if the quantum state is a description of reality or a way to place bets."

## Bell's Theorem and Entanglement

Einstein's 1935 challenge to quantum mechanics — proposing hidden variables might restore classical realism — was ultimately refuted. Physicist John Bell's subsequent tests demonstrated that particles exhibit mysterious correlations across space inconsistent with Einstein's expectations.

This means either: (1) particles lack definite properties before measurement, or (2) influences travel instantaneously across space, violating relativity.

## New Research Directions

Several physicists pursue novel approaches:

**De les Coves and Spekkens** have demonstrated that classical universes — where particles genuinely possess fixed positions — could appear quantum to observers trapped inside them. If true, ψ's multiple possibilities might represent our ignorance rather than physical reality.

**Gravity's Role:** Some researchers suspect quantum mechanics' meaning will only clarify through integration with general relativity. When spacetime itself becomes quantized, causality and temporal ordering become ambiguous.

## Ongoing Uncertainty

Despite a century of development, quantum mechanics remains philosophically unsettled. As one participant noted: "This was the first conference I've been to where people spoke openly and honestly about quantum mechanics missing something."

The conference concluded with cautious optimism. One physicist observed: "We're privileged to live at a time when the great prize of making sense of quantum theory is still there for the taking, and any one of us could take it."

The departure from Helgoland was considerably smoother than the arrival, with blue skies and calm waters replacing the rough North Sea crossing — though the conceptual turbulence surrounding quantum mechanics' interpretation shows no signs of settling.
