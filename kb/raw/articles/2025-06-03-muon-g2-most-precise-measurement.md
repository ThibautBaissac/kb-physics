# Muon g-2 Announces Most Precise Measurement of the Magnetic Anomaly of the Muon

The Muon g-2 experiment at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory has released its third and final measurement of the muon's magnetic anomaly, achieving a precision of 127 parts-per-billion — exceeding the original design goal of 140 parts-per-billion.

The final experimental value is:

**aμ = (g-2)/2 (muon, experiment) = 0.001 165 920 705 ± 0.000 000 000 114(stat.) ± 0.000 000 000 091(syst.)**

This result combines data from six years of measurements (2018–2023) and "agrees with their published results from 2021 and 2023, but with a much better precision."

## Key Findings

The measurement aligns perfectly with previous results, solidifying the experimental world average. Muons — particles roughly 200 times heavier than electrons — possess a quantum property called spin that creates a magnetic moment. When placed in an external magnetic field, this internal magnet wobbles or precesses like a spinning top.

The experiment tests the Standard Model of particle physics by comparing theoretical predictions against experimental observations. Recently, new theoretical calculations using computational approaches (the BMW lattice QCD calculation) have narrowed the discrepancy between theory and experiment, reducing evidence for new physics beyond the Standard Model.

## Precision Achievement

Physicist Peter Winter noted: "We not only achieved our goals but exceeded them, which is not very easy for these precision measurements."

Cornell professor Lawrence Gibbons reflected that "g-2 has been teaching us a lot about the nature of nature" over nearly a century, referencing that the study of the electron's g-factor dates back to early quantum mechanics.

## Collaboration

The collaboration comprises 176 scientists from 34 institutions across seven countries. The experiment uniquely combines expertise from high-energy physicists, accelerator physicists, atomic physicists, and nuclear physicists — a rare multidisciplinary collaboration.

## Theoretical Context

The so-called "anomalous magnetic moment" of the muon — denoted aμ — is the deviation of the muon's magnetic moment from the Dirac equation prediction of exactly g=2. Virtual particles from all sectors of the Standard Model contribute to this value, making it an extraordinarily sensitive probe of BSM physics. For decades, the experimental world average had hovered approximately 3–4σ above the Standard Model prediction, fueling excitement about possible new physics. The new BMW lattice QCD calculations have brought the theoretical prediction closer to the experimental value, making the status of the discrepancy less clear.
