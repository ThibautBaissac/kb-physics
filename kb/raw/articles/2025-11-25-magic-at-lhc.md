# Particle Physicists Detect 'Magic' at the Large Hadron Collider

Ninety million times a year, when protons crash together at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), they produce a top quark and an anti-top quark — the heaviest known elementary particles. In the trillionth of a trillionth of a second before decay, these particles fly apart while remaining quantum mechanically entangled. If the top quark spins in one direction, the anti-top quark must spin the opposite way.

Top quarks are special because they decay before combining with other quarks, allowing the LHC's detectors to record their states through the particles they produce. The ATLAS experiment measured entanglement between top and anti-top quarks for the first time in 2023, followed by additional measurements.

Seventeen years after the LHC's startup, particle physicists are using the collider to explore how information flows through quantum systems — a foundational question for quantum computing. The two possible quark spins correspond to the 0 and 1 states of a qubit. "It is treating the process of colliding things together and forming new particles as a quantum processor," noted Alan Barr from the University of Oxford.

Regina Demina from the University of Rochester described this convergence as "really an emergent field" and "like a gold rush right now."

## A Little Bit of Magic

Quantum computers can run certain algorithms exponentially faster than regular computers through entanglement, which links different qubits in contingent possibility networks. However, the 1990s Gottesman-Knill theorem revealed that certain highly entangled states called stabilizer states can be simulated equally efficiently on classical computers.

Physicists then sought entangled states differing maximally from stabilizer states — named "magic states." ("It's an appalling word," admitted Martin White, though changing it seems unlikely after 20 years.)

In 2014, physicists identified the crucial element: contextuality — a lesser-known quantum mechanics feature where measurement outcomes depend on simultaneously measured properties. Stabilizer states are noncontextual exceptions, but magic states require contextuality, making them classically difficult to simulate.

Martin and Chris White, identical twins from the University of Adelaide and Queen Mary University of London respectively, wondered whether the LHC — itself a quantum system — could reveal magic in top quarks. "I found it really quite emotional when it was released. We wanted to work together for many years," Martin White reflected on their collaboration.

When Demina met the brothers at a conference, she was inspired to propose the measurement to her CMS group. She observed: "They are identical twins...moved very far apart, but are still in an entangled state."

To measure top quark magic, CMS analyzed collision data, tallying spins of top quark pairs flying in different directions. This filled out a spin correlation matrix describing spin correlations in x, y, and z directions, from which physicists calculate magic.

The entangled quark pairs indeed possessed magic, marking quantum computing's entry into particle physics. However, the sensitive measurement methods revealed something unexpected: top quarks and anti-top quarks sometimes exhibited extra-entanglement while binding into toponium — an elusive state predicted in 1990 but "was thought to be a too-subtle effect" for LHC detection, according to Marcel Vos, ATLAS's top quark research leader.

CMS and ATLAS posted toponium measurements in March and July respectively. "That's our first tangible spin-off from all this," Vos said.

## Threads To Pull

The overlap between particle physics and quantum information theory excites physicists about using the LHC for subtle entanglement questions.

Marcel Vos asked: "What happens to your entangled system after the top quark decays? Will the daughters of the top quark still be entangled with the anti-top quark?" While quantum field theory predicts they should be, "no one's ever tested it."

These experiments might illuminate the quantum-to-classical transition — how uncertain quantum objects become definitively measured. When top quarks decay, they appear to "choose" spin direction, with decay particles traveling accordingly. "Mathematically, it's an equivalent process to making a measurement," explained Barr, offering fresh perspective on this quantum mystery.

Demina explores temporal questions, noting: "There is a certain theory that suggests that time is not a fundamental property of nature, but it is an emergent property." Don Page and William Wootters described a famous mechanism in 1983 where the universe appears timeless overall, while internal observers perceive temporal evolution through entangled spatial configurations. Demonstrated with photons in 2013, Demina dreams of "performing this experiment in a system of elementary particles, to demonstrate the Page-Wootters mechanism."

However, some physicists raise concerns about reliability. Herbert Dreiner from the University of Bonn argued in recent preprints that the approach is circular: measuring entanglement requires relating outgoing decay products' angular motion to top quarks' spins. "In order to translate one into the other, you have to use some theory...if you're using quantum mechanics, you can't test for quantum mechanics," Dreiner stated.

This debate continues. To some observers, after 17 years of LHC experiments, these new goals represent necessary evolution. Martin White reflected: "There is a sense that you're always looking for new things to do." While skepticism exists, Vos concluded: "You start pulling on the thread, and you don't know what you're going to come up with."
