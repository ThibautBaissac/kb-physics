# Into the quantum realm

The macroscopic realm, which consists of everything from falling balls to orbiting planets, can be explained by the laws of classical mechanics. When nature reaches the smallest scales, however, stranger, quantum rules kick in. Here, particles begin to exhibit bizarre properties: They do not have definite positions, and they can remain connected across vast distances and be altered by observation alone. To truly comprehend how the universe works, scientists need to be able to tap into this quantum realm.

One of the first to propose this idea was physicist Richard Feynman, who, in a now-famous 1981 lecture, stated that to understand the universe, scientists would need to use a quantum simulator. "Nature isn't classical—and if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical" Feynman said. A quantum computer capable of simulating high-energy physics phenomena has yet to be developed, but scientists have managed to build simulators that can address physics problems of the quantum realm.

Zohreh Davoudi, a theoretical physicist at the University of Maryland, notes "Several groups are pushing to reach the milestones that we have set in enabling truly quantum simulations of nature."

The weirdness of quantum phenomena makes them challenging to study. Dorota Grabowska, a theoretical physicist at the University of Washington, explains that quantum mechanics is probabilistic rather than deterministic: "You can determine the probabilities perfectly, but you can't determine what will actually happen perfectly."

Physicists currently tackle quantum problems on classical computers using the path integral formulation, which sums all possible paths a particle might take between two points. However, this method falls short when studying how quantum processes unfold over time. "Classically, we cannot address these problems because we run into an exponentially large problem size" Davoudi explains.

To understand quantum processes with many possibilities, scientists treat particles as waves representing multiple possible places and states simultaneously. This requires Schrödinger's equation, also known as the Hamiltonian formulation, which describes how probability changes over time. However, you cannot run Schrödinger's equation on a classical computer without exponentially larger memory and processing times than existing machines provide; a quantum computer is necessary.

Classical computers run on bits with values of 0 or 1. Quantum computers run on "qubits," which can be 0, 1, or in superposition of both states simultaneously. This allows quantum computers to process massive quantities of data rapidly, but interacting with a quantum system can change its properties and cause it to collapse into a classical system. "The difficulty with quantum computers is you have to interact with them. But you can't interact with them too much, or else they stop being quantum" Grabowska states.

Current quantum computers are small, typically containing a couple hundred qubits. Much larger machines are needed for most physics problems researchers hope to solve. Grabowska notes: "We are still quite limited in what we can do on quantum machines. But we're on the cusp of looking at the real-time properties of quantum systems that weren't accessible previously."

Over the past year, several groups have published studies of real-time string breaking, which describes what happens when pulling apart subatomic particles bound by the strong force releases a new particle-antiparticle pair. Studies used analog and digital quantum computers to simulate string-breaking in simplified models. "Qualitatively, the string-breaking mechanisms were the same between all three experiments" Davoudi states, noting this provides verification across different quantum platforms.

Researchers are also working on using quantum computers to analyze experimental data linked with quantum phenomena, such as particle collisions. Sofia Vallecorsa, coordinator of the Quantum Technology Initiative at CERN, explains that while such processes are inherently quantum mechanical, the collected data themselves—gathered using classical detectors—are not. The question is whether quantum computers can recover the original quantum mechanical laws from this data. Vallecorsa reports: "The good news is that they can, and for the different tasks that we have tried quantum algorithms on so far, we prove that they actually have good performance."

Studying quantum systems with quantum computers requires translating classical codes based on the path integral formulation into quantum ones rooted in Schrödinger's equation. Grabowska reflects: "To me it feels like a completely different way of thinking, which is exciting. It means that you're having to think about the same problems in completely different ways—and that is sometimes when breakthroughs happen."

The ultimate goal is creating a quantum supercomputer, a "fault-tolerant" machine free from current simulator limitations. Davoudi emphasizes: "No matter how far we take classical computing resources, we know that some physics problems cannot be addressed. Therefore, we need to be patient. Our best bet is to wait for this technology, and in the meantime, take advantage of the technology we have today."

Davoudi expresses optimism about progress: "The progress we've seen in hardware over the past five to 10 years has been really breathtaking. I'm very hopeful that in 10 years, we'll be in a place that we could not predict today."
