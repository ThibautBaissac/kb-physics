# A Single, 'Naked' Black Hole Rewrites the History of the Universe

The James Webb Space Telescope has discovered an extraordinary black hole in the early universe that challenges fundamental assumptions about cosmic history. This object, designated QSO1, weighs approximately 50 million suns yet exists without a surrounding galaxy—a phenomenon that contradicts conventional models of how the universe developed.

## The Discovery

Astronomer Lukas Furtak at Ben-Gurion University first identified QSO1 while examining gravitational lens imagery from JWST in 2023. The black hole appeared as three tiny red dots in the image, distinguishable from galaxies because they showed no signs of distortion despite the gravitational lensing effect.

Over six months, Furtak's team conducted spectroscopic analysis on each of the three images, spending 40 hours on each observation. Their findings confirmed QSO1 as "a glowing black hole packing a mass of tens of millions of suns," existing when the universe was merely 750 million years old.

## The Puzzle

Roberto Maiolino from the University of Cambridge led subsequent research in 2024, using JWST for additional 10-hour observations. His team achieved pixel-level spectral analysis, measuring gas movement around the black hole to directly calculate its mass: 50 million solar masses.

The analysis revealed something startling: no parent galaxy surrounds QSO1. The gas orbits the central point much like Earth orbits the sun, indicating matter is compressed into an incredibly dense form. The black hole comprises at least two-thirds of QSO1's total mass, with the remaining portion being gas and possibly some stars.

Additionally, the orbiting gas is essentially pure hydrogen—primordial material unchanged since the Big Bang. This purity suggests the black hole formed before nearby stars had lived and died, which would have scattered heavier elements throughout the region.

## Theoretical Explanations

The discovery has reignited debate about black hole origins. Several competing theories attempt to explain QSO1:

**Primordial Black Holes:** Stephen Hawking's 1971 proposal suggests the Big Bang itself created black holes from density fluctuations in the early universe. These "primordial" black holes could have grown over hundreds of millions of years to reach QSO1's current size.

**Not-Quite-Primordial Black Holes:** Researchers led by Wenzer Qin at New York University propose that dense regions formed but didn't immediately collapse. Over hundreds of thousands of years, these evolved into gas clouds too massive to become stars, eventually collapsing directly into black holes.

**Runaway Growth:** Priyamvada Natarajan at Yale University and Tal Alexander at the Weizmann Institute suggest a scenario where one star in a dense region collapses into a large black hole that rapidly consumes surrounding gas and stars, then persists alone after the other stars exhaust themselves.

**Direct Collapse:** Some researchers propose that gas clouds, prevented from cooling and fragmenting by residual Big Bang radiation, collapsed directly into black holes without the intermediate stellar stage.

## Broader Context

QSO1 represents one of over 300 "little red dots" that JWST has identified in the early universe. These objects exhibit characteristics of glowing black holes in some ways while appearing unusual in others, creating confusion about their true nature.

According to Dale Kocevski, an astronomer at Colby College: "Very rarely do you find things you can't explain." The discovery suggests the young universe was more chaotic and populated with giant black holes than previously understood.

John Regan from Maynooth University notes these objects "tell us we don't know anything" about early cosmic history, though he finds this "really exciting and very electrifying for the field."

## Implications

The conventional model—where stars collapse and merge to form supermassive black holes—faces timing challenges. By the universe's first billion years, observations reveal supermassive black holes anchoring galaxies, yet the growth process through stellar collapse appears too slow to achieve such massive objects in that timeframe.

QSO1 demonstrates that alternative formation mechanisms must exist. The object's isolation and composition suggest black holes may have preceded galaxies, fundamentally altering our understanding of cosmic development.

Marta Volonteri from the Paris Institute of Astrophysics states: "The most plausible explanation seems to be [that] the black hole developed before the galaxy."

## The Path Forward

Astrophysicists now face the challenge of understanding how QSO1 and similar objects evolved into the supermassive black holes observed at the centers of mature galaxies. The discovery has generated substantial theoretical interest, with competing models attempting to explain origins without definitive consensus.

The situation resembles, in Regan's metaphor, developing theories about human development by studying only adults and teenagers, then discovering toddlers—"messy new entities that are hard for researchers to interpret based on what they've seen before."
