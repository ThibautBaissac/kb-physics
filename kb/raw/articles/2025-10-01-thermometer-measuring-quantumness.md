# A Thermometer for Measuring Quantumness

If there's one law of physics that seems easy to grasp, it's the second law of thermodynamics: Heat flows spontaneously from hotter bodies to colder ones. But now, gently and almost casually, Alexssandre de Oliveira Jr. has just shown that this understanding may be incomplete.

Consider a hot cup of coffee and a cold jug of milk, the Brazilian physicist explained while sitting in a café in Copenhagen. When brought into contact, heat will flow from the hot object to the cold one, just as Rudolf Clausius formally stated in 1850. However, in some cases, quantum mechanics can drive heat flow in the opposite direction: from cold to hot.

This doesn't mean the second law fails, he clarified. Rather, Clausius' expression represents the "classical limit" of a more complete formulation required by quantum physics.

## Knowledge Is Power

The connection between the second law of thermodynamics and information was first explored in the 19th century by Scottish physicist James Clerk Maxwell. Clausius' second law seemed to imply that heat pockets will dissipate throughout the universe until all temperature differences vanish. In this process, the total entropy of the universe—a measure of disorder—would inexorably increase.

Maxwell realized this trend would eventually eliminate all possibility of harnessing heat flows for useful work. The universe would settle into a sterile equilibrium pervaded by uniform thermal motion: "heat death." This forecast troubled the devoutly Christian Maxwell. But in an 1867 letter to his friend Peter Guthrie Tait, Maxwell claimed to have found a way to "pick a hole" in the second law.

He imagined a tiny being (later dubbed a demon) who could see individual molecular motions in a gas. The gas would fill a box divided by a wall with a trapdoor. By selectively opening and closing the trapdoor, the demon could sequester faster-moving molecules in one compartment and slower-moving ones in another, creating hot and cold gases respectively. By acting on information about molecular motions, the demon could reduce the gas's entropy, creating a temperature gradient usable to do mechanical work like pushing a piston.

Scientists were convinced Maxwell's demon couldn't truly violate the second law, but it took nearly 100 years to understand why. The answer emerged through understanding that the information the demon collects and stores about molecular motions will eventually fill its finite memory. This memory must be erased and reset for it to continue working.

Physicist Rolf Landauer showed in 1961 that this erasure burns energy and produces entropy—more entropy than the demon's sorting reduces. Landauer's analysis established an equivalence between information and entropy, implying that information itself acts as a thermodynamic resource that can be converted into work. Physicists experimentally demonstrated this information-to-energy conversion in 2010.

However, quantum phenomena allow information processing in ways classical physics does not permit. This distinction is the entire basis of quantum computing and quantum cryptography technologies. Consequently, quantum theory complicates the conventional second law.

## Exploiting Correlations

Entangled quantum objects possess mutual information: they are correlated, allowing discovery of one object's properties by observing the other. This differs from classical correlation like paired gloves. While a glove's handedness is predetermined before observation, entangled quantum particles work differently according to quantum mechanics. Before measurement, only probabilities for possible observable property combinations can be known, such as 50% left-right and 50% right-left configurations. Only measurement resolves these possibilities into definite outcomes, destroying entanglement in the process.

If gas molecules are entangled, a Maxwell's demon can manipulate them more efficiently than independent molecules. If the demon knows that any fast-moving molecule it observes will be correlated with another fast one arriving moments later, it needn't observe the second particle before opening the trapdoor. The thermodynamic cost of temporarily circumventing the second law decreases.

In 2004, quantum theorists Caslav Brukner of the University of Vienna and Vlatko Vedral, then at Imperial College London, pointed out that macroscopic thermodynamic measurements can serve as a "witness" revealing quantum entanglement between particles. Under certain conditions, a system's heat capacity or magnetic field response should bear entanglement's imprint if present.

Similarly, other physicists calculated that more work can be extracted from a warm body when quantum entanglement exists in the system compared to purely classical scenarios.

In 2008, physicist Hossein Partovi of California State University identified a particularly dramatic implication of how quantum entanglement undermines classical thermodynamics preconceptions. He realized that entanglement's presence can actually reverse spontaneous heat flow from a hot object to a cold one, seemingly overturning the second law itself.

That reversal represents a special refrigeration type, according to Nicole Yunger Halpern. Like all refrigeration, it isn't free. Classically, refrigerating an object requires work—heat must be pumped the "wrong" way by consuming fuel, thereby repaying entropy lost by making the cold object colder and the hot object hotter. In the quantum case, instead of burning fuel for refrigeration, "you burn the correlations." As anomalous heat flow proceeds, entanglement gets destroyed: particles with initially correlated properties become independent. "Correlations can be used as a resource to push heat in the opposite direction," Halpern explained.

In effect, the fuel here is information itself: specifically the mutual information of the entangled hot and cold bodies.

Two years later, David Jennings and Terry Rudolph of Imperial College London clarified what occurs. They showed how the second law can be reformulated to include cases where mutual information is present, and they calculated limits on how much the classical heat flow can be altered and even reversed by consuming quantum correlations.

## The Demon Knows

When quantum effects are in play, the second law isn't so simple. But can anything useful be done with quantum physics loosening thermodynamic law bounds? That's one goal of quantum thermodynamics, where researchers seek quantum engines running more efficiently than classical ones, or quantum batteries charging more quickly.

Patryk Lipka-Bartosik of the Center for Theoretical Physics at the Polish Academy of Sciences pursued practical applications differently: using thermodynamics as a tool for probing quantum physics. Last year, he and co-workers saw how to realize Brukner and Vedral's 2004 idea of using thermodynamic properties as quantum entanglement witnesses. Their scheme involves hot and cold quantum systems correlated with each other, and a third system mediating heat flow between them. This third system can be thought of as a Maxwell's demon, except it now possesses a "quantum memory" that can itself be entangled with the systems it manipulates. Being entangled with the demon's memory effectively links the hot and cold systems so the demon can infer something about one from the other's properties.

Such a quantum demon acts as a kind of catalyst, helping heat transfer happen by accessing correlations that would otherwise be inaccessible. Because it is entangled with the hot and cold objects, the demon can divine and exploit all their correlations systematically. Like a classical catalyst, this third system returns to its original state once heat exchange between the objects completes. This process can boost anomalous heat flow beyond what's achievable without such a catalyst.

The paper this year by de Oliveira, co-authored by Lipka-Bartosik and Jonatan Bohr Brask of the Technical University of Denmark, uses similar ideas but with a crucial difference transforming the setup into a thermometer for measuring quantumness. In earlier work, the demonlike quantum memory interacted with a correlated pair of quantum systems, one hot and one cold. In the latest work, it sits between a quantum system (such as an array of entangled quantum bits, or qubits, in a quantum computer) and a simple heat sink with which the quantum system is not directly entangled.

Because the memory is entangled with both the quantum system and the sink, it can again catalyze heat flow between them beyond what's classically possible. In that process, entanglement within the quantum system converts into extra heat entering the sink. Measuring the energy stored in the heat sink reveals the presence of entanglement in the quantum system. But since the system and sink aren't themselves entangled, the measurement doesn't affect the quantum system's state. This approach circumvents the notorious way measurements destroy quantumness. "If you simply tried to make a measurement on the system directly, you'd destroy its entanglement before the process could even unfold," de Oliveira said.

The new scheme offers advantages of simplicity and generality, according to Vedral, now at the University of Oxford. "These verification protocols are very important," he stated: Whenever quantum computer companies announce new device performance, the question arises of how (or if) they know that qubit entanglement actually helps with computation. A heat sink could serve as a quantum phenomenon detector purely through its energy change. To implement the idea, you might designate one quantum bit as the memory whose state reveals other qubits' states, then couple this memory qubit to particles serving as the sink, whose energy you can measure. One requirement, Vedral noted, is having very good system control to ensure no other heat flow sources contaminate measurements. Another is that the method won't detect all entangled states.

De Oliveira believes a system already exists for testing their idea experimentally. He and colleagues are discussing this goal with Roberto Serra's research group at the Federal University of ABC in Sao Paulo, Brazil. In 2016, Serra and colleagues used carbon and hydrogen atom magnetic orientations, or spins, in chloroform molecules as quantum bits between which they could transfer heat.

Using this setup, de Oliveira says it should be possible to exploit a quantum behavior—in this case coherence, meaning two or more spins' properties evolve in phase with one another—to change heat flow between atoms. Qubit coherence is essential for quantum computing, so verifying it by detecting anomalous heat exchange could prove helpful.

The stakes could be even higher. Several research groups are designing experiments to determine whether gravity is a quantum force like the other three fundamental forces. Some efforts involve looking for quantum entanglement between two objects generated purely by their mutual gravitational attraction. Perhaps researchers could probe such gravity-induced entanglement by making simple thermodynamic measurements on them—thereby verifying (or not) that gravity is truly quantized.

Studying one of physics' deepest questions, Vedral remarked, "wouldn't it be lovely if you could do something as easy and macroscopic as this?"
