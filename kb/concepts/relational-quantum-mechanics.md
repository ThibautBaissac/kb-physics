# Relational Quantum Mechanics

Relational quantum mechanics (RQM), proposed by [Carlo Rovelli](../people/carlo-rovelli.md) in 1996, is an interpretation of quantum theory in which physical properties — including the values of measured quantities — are not absolute facts about objects in isolation but facts about *relations* between systems.

## Core Idea

In standard quantum mechanics, measurement causes the [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) to collapse, yielding a definite outcome. The [measurement problem](../open-questions/measurement-problem.md) is that the theory provides no mechanism for this: the Schrödinger equation is linear and deterministic and never collapses anything.

RQM resolves this by denying that the quantum state is an absolute description. The quantum state of system B, relative to system A (the observer), is well-defined. But system C — a second observer who hasn't interacted with A — may assign a different quantum state to the same situation. This is not a contradiction; both descriptions are correct relative to their respective observers.

Collapse is not a physical event but a relational fact: from A's perspective, B's state became definite when they interacted. From C's perspective (before C interacts with A or B), B's state is still in superposition. Both are correct.

## Relationship to Entanglement

In RQM, [quantum entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) is the fundamental expression of the relational nature of reality. All interactions are entanglement events. The correlations between entangled systems are not absolute facts but facts about how the systems relate to each other. Bell's theorem's demand for nonlocality is absorbed into the relational structure: there are no pre-existing absolute properties to correlate across space.

## Similarities and Differences

- **vs. Copenhagen:** Copenhagen also denies that ψ describes reality independently of observation, but it relies on an unspecified "classical" measuring apparatus. RQM is more general: any physical system can be an observer, and no special classical/quantum cut is needed.
- **vs. QBism:** [QBism](../concepts/qbism.md) restricts the relational framework to agents with subjective experience. RQM is fully objective in a relational sense — any physical system plays the role of observer, with no appeal to subjectivity.
- **vs. Many-Worlds:** Many-Worlds posits that all outcomes occur in branching universes. RQM denies branching; instead, the outcome is definite relative to the interacting system, but nothing more.

## Connection to Quantum Gravity

Rovelli's RQM is philosophically aligned with his work on loop quantum gravity, where spacetime itself is relational — there is no background spacetime independent of the fields it contains. Both theories reflect a conviction that physics should be written in terms of interactions and relations, not in terms of absolute objects and states.
