# Cosmological Horizon

A cosmological horizon is the boundary in [de Sitter space](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md) beyond which an observer can never receive signals, because the exponential expansion of space outpaces the speed of light. It is analogous to a black hole's event horizon, but with key differences.

## How It Forms

In de Sitter space, the exponential expansion creates a "current" too powerful for any signal to overcome. Monica Pate (NYU) explains: "It's expanding so fast that there are parts of space-time which, if you wait forever, you will never be able to see." If our universe continues to expand as predicted, everything beyond our cosmological horizon will remain permanently out of reach — as if we are trapped inside a black hole.

## Comparison with Black Hole Horizons

Both black hole and cosmological horizons define boundaries of causal disconnection, but they differ in important ways:

- A **black hole** has a single, observer-independent horizon.
- **De Sitter space** has many horizons, each centered on a different observer.

This multiplicity of horizons is a central obstacle for extending [holography](../concepts/holography.md) to de Sitter space. Without a single boundary to anchor calculations, [Tom Hartman](../people/tom-hartman.md) (Stanford) notes that formulating a quantum theory of de Sitter space yields something paradoxically empty: "There's some sense in which it wants to not have any states in it."
