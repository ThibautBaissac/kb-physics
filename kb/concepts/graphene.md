# Graphene

Graphene is a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a honeycomb lattice. Discovered in 2004 by Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov — who peeled it off pencil lead using Scotch tape, earning a Nobel Prize — graphene is remarkable for its atomic purity.

## Role in Electron Hydrodynamics

Graphene is "a thermodynamically beautiful crystal" that "comes out of the earth well formed, with very few impurities," according to [Cory Dean](../people/cory-dean.md) (Columbia). This purity is what makes it the key material for [electron hydrodynamics](../concepts/electron-hydrodynamics.md): with almost no impurities to scatter off, electrons in graphene collide primarily with each other, conserving momentum and entering a fluid-like regime.

It took about a decade after graphene's discovery for physicists to learn how to study it without interference from surrounding materials. Once they did, they confirmed the Gurzhi effect (2017) and eventually produced supersonic electron shock waves (2025). See [electron fluid experiments](../experiments/electron-fluid-experiments.md).
