# Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement is the phenomenon in which two or more quantum particles, after interacting via physical forces, become described by a single [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) rather than as independent entities. Measuring one particle appears to instantaneously influence the other, regardless of the distance between them.

Schrödinger named the phenomenon in 1935, calling it the key feature of quantum mechanics. Einstein and colleagues had pointed out the strange interconnection, but quantum mechanics shows that entangled particles are simply no longer separate entities — they share a joint quantum state.

## Ubiquity

Entanglement is inevitable whenever particles interact. This has profound consequences: any quantum object interacting with its environment — photons bouncing off an apple, air molecules colliding with a dust grain — becomes entangled with that environment. Entanglement is not exotic; it is the universal information conduit between quantum and classical worlds.

## Role in Decoherence

[Wojciech Zurek](../people/wojciech-zurek.md) and H. Dieter Zeh recognized that ubiquitous entanglement is what drives [decoherence](../concepts/decoherence.md). As a quantum object becomes entangled with its environment, the quantum superposition becomes shared across all the entangled entities. The object's quantumness is effectively "diluted" into the environment, making it unobservable in the original system.

## Role in Measurement

Entanglement with a measuring device is what constitutes a quantum measurement. The quantum object under observation becomes entangled with the atoms of the instrument, carrying information from the quantum realm to the classical world. [Quantum Darwinism](../concepts/quantum-darwinism.md) formalizes how robust "pointer states" generate multiple identical imprints via entanglement.
