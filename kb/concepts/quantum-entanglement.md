# Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement is the phenomenon in which two or more quantum particles, after interacting via physical forces, become described by a single [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) rather than as independent entities. Measuring one particle appears to instantaneously influence the other, regardless of the distance between them.

Schrödinger named the phenomenon in 1935, calling it the key feature of quantum mechanics. Einstein and colleagues had pointed out the strange interconnection, but quantum mechanics shows that entangled particles are simply no longer separate entities — they share a joint quantum state.

## Ubiquity

Entanglement is inevitable whenever particles interact. This has profound consequences: any quantum object interacting with its environment — photons bouncing off an apple, air molecules colliding with a dust grain — becomes entangled with that environment. Entanglement is not exotic; it is the universal information conduit between quantum and classical worlds.

## Role in Decoherence

[Wojciech Zurek](../people/wojciech-zurek.md) and H. Dieter Zeh recognized that ubiquitous entanglement is what drives [decoherence](../concepts/decoherence.md). As a quantum object becomes entangled with its environment, the quantum superposition becomes shared across all the entangled entities. The object's quantumness is effectively "diluted" into the environment, making it unobservable in the original system.

## Role in Measurement

Entanglement with a measuring device is what constitutes a quantum measurement. The quantum object under observation becomes entangled with the atoms of the instrument, carrying information from the quantum realm to the classical world. [Quantum Darwinism](../concepts/quantum-darwinism.md) formalizes how robust "pointer states" generate multiple identical imprints via entanglement.

## Entanglement at the LHC

The [Large Hadron Collider](../experiments/large-hadron-collider.md) produces entangled top quark and anti-top quark pairs ninety million times per year. Because top quarks decay before forming hadrons, their entangled states are directly observable through decay products — making the LHC a unique laboratory for studying entanglement in the most massive known particles.

ATLAS first measured top-antitop entanglement in 2023. Subsequent measurements revealed [magic states](../concepts/magic-states.md) — entangled states with [contextuality](../concepts/contextuality.md) that cannot be simulated classically — and led to the unexpected discovery of [toponium](../concepts/toponium.md). The [Page-Wootters mechanism](../concepts/page-wootters-mechanism.md), where time emerges from entanglement, has been proposed for testing with these systems.

## Entanglement and Thermodynamics

Entanglement has deep consequences for thermodynamics. When two bodies are entangled, heat can flow spontaneously from cold to hot — seemingly violating Clausius' second law. The "fuel" for this anomalous heat flow is the entanglement itself: as heat flows the wrong way, the correlations are consumed and destroyed. This makes entanglement a thermodynamic resource, on par with temperature gradients or chemical potential differences.

This connection enables a practical application: using thermodynamic measurements as non-destructive detectors of entanglement. [Alexssandre de Oliveira Jr.](../people/alexssandre-de-oliveira.md) and colleagues (2025) showed that a quantum memory mediating between an entangled system and a heat sink can convert entanglement into measurable heat — without disturbing the quantum state. See [quantum thermodynamics](../concepts/quantum-thermodynamics.md) for details.

## Barrier to Classical Simulation

Entanglement is the fundamental reason classical computers cannot simulate quantum fields. As particles interact and become entangled, their collective mathematical description grows exponentially. [Mikhail Lukin](../people/mikhail-lukin.md) (Harvard): "At some point it exponentially explodes. You run out of memory on a classical computer." This motivates the development of [quantum simulation](../concepts/quantum-simulation.md) — using quantum hardware with entanglement "baked in" to simulate quantum fields tractably.

## Bell's Theorem and Nonlocality

[Bell's theorem](../concepts/bells-theorem.md), proved by [John Bell](../people/john-bell.md) in 1964, established that no local hidden-variable theory can reproduce all quantum predictions. Entangled particles exhibit correlations that cannot be explained by pre-existing properties — either measurements create outcomes (quantum indeterminism) or distant measurements influence each other instantaneously (nonlocality). This result is central to the [measurement problem](../open-questions/measurement-problem.md): any interpretation must explain or accommodate Bell nonlocality.

## Bell Tests and Complex Numbers

Bell tests — where entangled particles are separated and measured by different participants — are a crucial probe of quantum theory's properties. In 2021, [Nicolas Gisin](../people/nicolas-gisin.md) extended the standard two-participant Bell test to three participants (Alice, Bob, and Charlie) with two entangled-photon sources. This extended test found correlations exceeding limits predicted by real-valued quantum theories, initially suggesting [complex numbers](complex-numbers-in-quantum-mechanics.md) are essential. However, 2025 work showed the test assumed a specific tensor product structure; generalized real-valued theories match all complex-valued predictions.
