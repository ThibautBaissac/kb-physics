# Magic States

Magic states are [entangled](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) quantum states that differ maximally from so-called stabilizer states. They are the essential resource that gives quantum computers their exponential advantage over classical machines for certain algorithms.

## Stabilizer States and the Gottesman-Knill Theorem

In the 1990s, the Gottesman-Knill theorem established a surprising limitation: certain highly entangled quantum states — called stabilizer states — can be efficiently simulated on a classical computer. Entanglement alone is therefore not sufficient for quantum advantage.

This raised a fundamental question: what *additional* property, beyond entanglement, makes quantum computation genuinely powerful?

## The Role of Contextuality

In 2014, physicists identified the crucial ingredient: [contextuality](../concepts/contextuality.md). Contextuality is the quantum mechanical feature where the outcome of a measurement depends on what other properties are being measured simultaneously. Stabilizer states are the noncontextual exceptions — they lack this property. Magic states require contextuality, which is what makes them impossible to simulate efficiently on classical computers.

## Detection at the LHC

[Martin White](../people/martin-white.md) (University of Adelaide) and [Chris White](../people/chris-white.md) (Queen Mary University of London) — identical twin physicists — proposed that the [Large Hadron Collider](../experiments/large-hadron-collider.md), as a quantum system producing entangled top quark pairs, could reveal magic. [Regina Demina](../people/regina-demina.md) (University of Rochester) brought the proposal to the CMS experiment.

CMS analyzed collision data by measuring spin correlations of top quark pairs flying in different directions, filling out a spin correlation matrix in the x, y, and z directions. The entangled quark pairs indeed possessed magic — marking the first detection of quantum magic in a particle physics experiment and opening quantum information research as a new frontier at the LHC.

The sensitive measurement methods also led to the unexpected discovery of [toponium](../concepts/toponium.md), a top-antitop quark bound state.

## Significance

The detection of magic at the LHC represents a convergence of particle physics and quantum information theory. As [Alan Barr](../people/alan-barr.md) (University of Oxford) explained, the LHC effectively treats "the process of colliding things together and forming new particles as a quantum processor." The two possible quark spin states correspond to the 0 and 1 states of a qubit.

Demina described the field as "really an emergent field" and "like a gold rush right now."
