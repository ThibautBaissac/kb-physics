# Holography

The holographic principle states that the information contained within a volume of space can be fully described by a theory living on the boundary of that region. The three-dimensional interior is, in a sense, illusory — like a hologram projected from a two-dimensional surface.

## Black Hole Holography

The principle originated from black hole physics. The two-dimensional surface (event horizon) of a black hole captures everything about the three-dimensional space inside it. This insight built on the Bekenstein-Hawking formula for [black hole entropy](../concepts/black-hole-entropy.md), which showed that a black hole's entropy is proportional to its horizon area, not its volume.

Holography has made black holes powerful settings for studying quantum gravity, since the extreme gravity acts strongly even on the quantum scale.

## AdS/CFT Correspondence

The most developed realization of holography is the AdS/CFT correspondence, which relates gravity in the bulk of [anti-de Sitter space](../concepts/anti-de-sitter-space.md) to a conformal field theory on its boundary. This duality has been a major tool in [string theory](../theories/string-theory.md) and quantum gravity research.

Anti-de Sitter space is especially amenable to holography because it has a well-defined conformal boundary where quantum fluctuations vanish, enabling precise calculations.

## Island Formula

In 2019, [Juan Maldacena](../people/juan-maldacena.md) and colleagues developed the [island formula](../concepts/island-formula.md), which tracks regions within black holes and helped explain how they release information. When applied to closed universes — spacetimes without boundaries — the formula reveals they have one-dimensional [Hilbert spaces](../concepts/hilbert-space.md) containing zero information, a paradox that may require observer-dependent boundaries to resolve.

## The De Sitter Challenge

Physicists hope to extend holography to [de Sitter space](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md), but face a fundamental obstacle: de Sitter space has no single accessible boundary. Instead, it has multiple observer-dependent [cosmological horizons](../concepts/cosmological-horizon.md).

[Tom Hartman](../people/tom-hartman.md) (Stanford) uses black hole holography as "a warm-up problem" for de Sitter cosmology: "Anytime we make progress on black holes, we go back and ask: Can we apply this to de Sitter?" But when applied, the results are puzzling — a de Sitter universe "wants to not have any states in it," suggesting current interpretations may be wrong.

Despite these difficulties, Hartman notes growing confidence that holography will eventually apply to de Sitter: "That's always been kind of believed, but I think in the last few years, it's become more convincing."

See [Quantum Gravity in de Sitter Space](../open-questions/quantum-gravity-in-de-sitter-space.md) for the broader open problem.
