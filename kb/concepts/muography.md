# Muography

Muography is a non-destructive imaging technique that uses natural cosmic-ray [muons](../concepts/muon.md) to visualize the interiors of large, dense structures. Because muons penetrate far deeper than X-rays or gamma rays, they enable imaging at scales impossible with other methods — from ancient monuments to active volcanoes.

## How It Works

Cosmic-ray muons shower Earth continuously. Denser or more massive material absorbs more muons; detectors placed on the far side record the shadow. By comparing muon flux distributions, scientists reconstruct a 3D density map of the structure.

A second method — **muon scattering tomography** — exploits the muon's electric charge. When a muon encounters an atomic nucleus, its electric field deflects the muon. By analyzing the statistical distribution of many deflection angles, scientists infer what materials are present — useful for detecting high-Z (heavy) materials like uranium or lead hidden in cargo.

## Applications

### Archaeology and Cultural Heritage
- **Great Pyramid of Giza (2023):** Scientists announced the discovery of a hidden corridor inside the 4,500-year-old pyramid using muon flux measurements from cosmic rays passing through it.
- **Ancient Chinese wall:** Interior structure mapped non-invasively.

### Volcanology
- **MURAVES (MUon RAdiography of VESuvius):** An ongoing project to image the internal structure of Mount Vesuvius, the active Italian volcano that buried Pompeii. Understanding its interior is key to eruption forecasting.

### Security and Nuclear
- **Nuclear reactors:** Muography can image reactor cores without disrupting operations.
- **Cargo scanning:** Muon scattering tomography can detect smuggled high-density materials (nuclear materials, contraband) inside containers.

## Origins in Particle Physics

The detectors used in muography are direct descendants of high-energy physics instruments. [Andrea Giammanco](../people/andrea-giammanco.md) (Université Catholique de Louvain) began exploring muon applications outside particle physics and found the range of uses "blew my mind." [Cristina Carloganu](../people/cristina-carloganu.md) (French National Institute of Nuclear and Particle Physics) frames muons as evidence that "research should not be hyperspecialized."
