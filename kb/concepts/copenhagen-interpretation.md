# Copenhagen Interpretation

<!-- stub: needs enrichment via kb:ingest -->

The Copenhagen interpretation, developed by [Werner Heisenberg](../people/werner-heisenberg.md) and Niels Bohr in the 1920s, treats quantum mechanics as a tool for predicting measurement outcomes rather than describing underlying reality. The [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) collapses upon measurement, yielding a definite classical result. Why collapse happens — and where the quantum-classical boundary lies ("Heisenberg's cut") — is left unexplained, motivating the [measurement problem](../open-questions/measurement-problem.md). The Copenhagen view is challenged by [QBism](../concepts/qbism.md), [Relational QM](../concepts/relational-quantum-mechanics.md), and the [many-worlds interpretation](../concepts/many-worlds-interpretation.md). [Zurek](../people/wojciech-zurek.md)'s [decoherence](../concepts/decoherence.md) program attempts to derive the appearance of collapse from standard quantum mechanics, potentially reconciling Copenhagen with many-worlds.
