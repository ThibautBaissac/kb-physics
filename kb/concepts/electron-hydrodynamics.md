# Electron Hydrodynamics

Electron hydrodynamics is a regime of electronic transport where electrons conserve momentum in collisions with each other — behaving collectively like a fluid rather than as independent particles bouncing off impurities.

## Dispersive vs. Hydrodynamic Flow

In a typical metal wire, electrons collide primarily with lattice vibrations and impurities, losing momentum at each collision. This "dispersive" flow is like water seeping through packed sand — each electron acts independently. [Andrew Lucas](../people/andrew-lucas.md) (Colorado Boulder) compares it to pinballs bouncing off bumpers.

In contrast, when electrons collide mainly with *each other* (as water molecules do), they conserve momentum like billiard balls. This enables collective behavior: faster- and slower-moving zones, swirling eddies, and viscous flow — the hallmarks of a fluid.

## The Gurzhi Effect

In 1963, Soviet physicist Radii Gurzhi first calculated the consequences of momentum-conserving electron collisions. He predicted an inversion of the usual temperature-resistance relationship: in a hydrodynamic electron fluid, heating should *decrease* resistance, similar to how warm honey flows more readily than cold honey. This counterintuitive "Gurzhi effect" remained a theoretical curiosity for decades, since real wires were too full of impurities.

## Why Graphene Changed Everything

[Graphene](../concepts/graphene.md) — an atomically pure honeycomb of carbon — provided the ultra-clean environment needed for electron hydrodynamics. With almost no impurities to scatter off, electrons in graphene interact primarily with each other, entering the hydrodynamic regime.

## Implications

Electron hydrodynamics offers two major opportunities:

1. **Novel electronics:** When electrons flow as fluids, device geometry matters — shapes like nozzles and cavities produce different physics. [Thomas Scaffidi](../people/thomas-scaffidi.md) (UC Irvine): "By using different shapes for your device, you can realize very different physics."

2. **New theoretical tools:** Hydrodynamic descriptions — density, viscosity, conservation laws — could provide a way to understand complex quantum materials without tracking every electron. Scaffidi calls this "a baby step" toward using fluid mechanics to understand quantum systems.
