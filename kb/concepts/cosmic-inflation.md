# Cosmic Inflation

Cosmic inflation is a period of rapid, exponential expansion in the very early universe. During inflation, the universe closely resembled [de Sitter space](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md) — a spacetime dominated by a positive [cosmological constant](../concepts/cosmological-constant.md) (or an effective equivalent driven by the inflaton field).

## Connection to De Sitter Space

Our universe likely looked a lot like de Sitter space during inflation. After inflation ended, the universe transitioned to a flatter geometry thanks to the presence of matter and radiation. But as expansion continues and matter becomes sparser, the universe is once again approaching de Sitter geometry.

This means de Sitter space bookends cosmic history: it approximates the geometry of both the very early and the very late universe.

## Starobinsky Inflation and Quadratic Gravity

The Soviet physicist Alexei Starobinsky used [quadratic gravity](../theories/quadratic-gravity.md) in 1980 to formulate the first theory of cosmic inflation. The scalar particle in quadratic gravity is exactly the type of field that could drive the explosive early-universe expansion. Starobinsky inflation predicts gravitational wave imprints on the sky too small for current telescopes to detect, but potentially within reach of next-generation observatories.

[John Donoghue](../people/john-donoghue.md): "Starobinsky inflation is the only remaining one that makes sense from a quantum field theory point of view."

Several other inflation models have been ruled out by the absence of predicted gravitational wave signatures.

## Early-Universe Density Fluctuations

Inflation's density fluctuations may have seeded the formation of [primordial black holes](../concepts/primordial-black-holes.md). JWST's discovery of massive early-universe black holes — including QSO1, a naked black hole at 750 million years — has reignited debate about whether such density spikes during or shortly after inflation could have directly produced the seeds of [supermassive black holes](../open-questions/supermassive-black-hole-origins.md).
