# Renormalization

Renormalization is the procedure that tames the infinities arising in [quantum field theory](../theories/quantum-field-theory.md). Quantum fields support an unending number of ripples at every scale, and naively summing over all of them produces infinite answers. Renormalization provides a way to extract finite, testable predictions.

## Discovery (Late 1940s)

Richard Feynman, Julian Schwinger, and Sin-Itiro Tomonaga independently discovered that the infinite-seeming parts of QFT calculations could be reexpressed in terms of a few measurable constants — the electron's mass and charge. This fixed the values of the divergent terms and allowed physicists to predict anything they liked about the electromagnetic field. The achievement earned all three the Nobel Prize.

## Physical Interpretation

Initially seen as an algebraic hack, renormalization gained a deep physical interpretation over the following decades. It amounts to a **filter** that blurs out the smallest ripples in a field and includes only their net effects. In a renormalizable theory, the tiny ripples have limited impact — the smaller they get, the less they influence the larger ripples. The blurry picture works well.

In a **non-renormalizable** theory, by contrast, every infinitesimal detail matters. The blurry picture looks quite different from the sharp picture. General relativity quantized naively is non-renormalizable: the tinier the spacetime ripples, the more they matter, and their effects cannot be summed up in terms of a few measurable constants.

## Gravity's Renormalization Problem

When Feynman and Bryce DeWitt attempted to renormalize gravity, they found that small spacetime ripples influence higher-level rippling in innumerable ways that resist being blurred out. This is why "quantum general relativity was considered a problem" ([John Donoghue](../people/john-donoghue.md)).

[Quadratic gravity](../theories/quadratic-gravity.md) solved this by adding curvature-squared terms that make spacetime ripples behave more like electromagnetic ripples — becoming less significant at smaller scales. This made gravity renormalizable for the first time, at the cost of introducing [ghost particles](../concepts/ghost-particles.md).
