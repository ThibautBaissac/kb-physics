# Gravitational Lensing

Gravitational lensing is the bending of light as it passes through spacetime warped by massive objects — a direct prediction of [general relativity](../theories/general-relativity.md). When light from a distant galaxy travels toward Earth, intervening mass (galaxy clusters, filaments of [dark matter](../concepts/dark-matter.md)) deflects its path. Observers on Earth see distorted, magnified, or multiplied images of the background source.

Because the lensing effect depends on the total mass along the line of sight — including invisible [dark matter](../concepts/dark-matter.md) — astrophysicists can invert the distortion pattern to map mass distributions that no telescope can directly image. As Theo Schutt (Stanford) explains: "We can use the gravitational lensing effect to measure the invisible dark matter by observing how distant galaxy images become distorted by intervening matter."

## Two Regimes

### Weak Lensing

When the alignment between observer, lens, and source is imperfect, lensing produces subtle, coherent shape distortions across large populations of galaxies. No individual galaxy appears dramatically warped, but statistical analysis of shape correlations across millions of objects reveals the underlying dark matter distribution. Weak lensing is the primary tool for large-scale dark matter mapping.

### Strong Lensing

When a background source aligns nearly perfectly behind a massive foreground object, the lensing is strong enough to create:
- Multiple images of the same source
- Arcs or rings (Einstein rings in perfect alignment)
- Dramatically magnified images

Strong lensing is used to study individual massive objects (galaxy clusters, compact dark matter concentrations) and to detect otherwise-too-faint objects like [supernovae](../concepts/supernova.md) in distant galaxies.

## Lensed Supernovae

A particularly powerful application: when a [supernova](../concepts/supernova.md) explodes behind a strong gravitational lens, its multiple lensed images arrive at Earth with different time delays determined by the geometry of the lens. Measuring these time delays provides an independent route to the Hubble constant — the rate of cosmic expansion — offering a check on tensions between other measurement methods.

Currently only a handful of lensed supernovae are known. The [Vera C. Rubin Observatory](../experiments/vera-c-rubin-observatory.md)'s LSST is expected to detect approximately 50 lensed supernovae per year — a roughly 100-fold increase over current rates.

## Scale Transformation from LSST

As of 2025, the gravitational lensing catalog stands at:
- ~1,000 known strong gravitational lens systems
- Hundreds of millions of galaxies with useful weak lensing shape measurements

The [Vera C. Rubin Observatory](../experiments/vera-c-rubin-observatory.md)'s LSST will transform these numbers to:
- **Tens of thousands** of strong lens systems
- **Billions** of weak lensing shape measurements

This expansion will require community-scale software infrastructure. Analysis pipelines are being prepared via simulations and open data challenges, and integration with complementary surveys (DESI, Euclid) will enhance constraining power on [dark energy](../concepts/dark-energy.md).
