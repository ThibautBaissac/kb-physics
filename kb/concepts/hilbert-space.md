# Hilbert Space

A Hilbert space is the abstract mathematical space used to describe quantum systems. Each dimension of the Hilbert space corresponds to additional information the system can carry. A classical bit has a two-dimensional Hilbert space (0 or 1); most quantum systems, including hydrogen atoms, possess infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces reflecting their unlimited possible states.

The [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) of a quantum system is a vector in its Hilbert space. The dimensionality of the Hilbert space determines how much information the system can encode.

## The Closed Universe Paradox

In 2019, [Juan Maldacena](../people/juan-maldacena.md) applied the [island formula](../concepts/island-formula.md) to closed universes — spacetimes with no edges or boundaries — and found that they possess **one-dimensional Hilbert spaces**. A one-dimensional Hilbert space means only one possible quantum state exists, containing zero bits of information.

[Edgar Shaghoulian](../people/edgar-shaghoulian.md) (UC Santa Cruz) found this paradoxical: "On my desk there are an infinite number of states." The result persisted across different theoretical models. [Henry Maxfield and Donald Marolf examined baby universes and found the same one-dimensional result.

As [Rob Myers](../people/rob-myers.md) (Perimeter Institute) notes: "We look around, and certainly the world seems more complex than that." This "empty Hilbert space" puzzle is a central challenge in [quantum gravity in de Sitter space](../open-questions/quantum-gravity-in-de-sitter-space.md). The resolution may require introducing an observer, whose presence expands the Hilbert space and restores the complexity we observe.

## Observers and Hilbert Space Enlargement

Shaghoulian noticed in 2023 that topological field theories also exhibit single-dimensional Hilbert spaces, but subdividing the space into zones causes the Hilbert space to enlarge. He proposed that introducing an observer into a closed universe acts analogously — the observer creates a boundary (around itself, not around the universe) that restores the full dimensionality.

[Ying Zhao](../people/ying-zhao.md), [Daniel Harlow](../people/daniel-harlow.md), and Mykhaylo Usatyuk (MIT) demonstrated this mathematically in January 2025: a classical observer inhabiting a closed universe restores the apparent complexity of the Hilbert space.
