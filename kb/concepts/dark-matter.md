# Dark Matter

Dark matter is an unknown form of matter that does not emit, absorb, or reflect light, yet exerts gravitational influence on visible matter, radiation, and the large-scale structure of the universe. As far as scientists know, only 15% of the matter in the universe is ordinary matter; the other 85% — dark matter — remains beyond direct detection, invisibly affecting the movements of galaxies and subtly warping our view of the stars.

Dark matter also permeates the local environment: it passes through us as we pass through it on Earth's journey through the cosmos.

## What Could It Be?

The fundamental nature of dark matter remains unknown. Candidates include:

- **WIMPs** (weakly interacting massive particles) — motivated by supersymmetry and the hierarchy problem; predicted to interact weakly with ordinary matter
- **Axions** — ultra-light particles originally proposed to solve the strong-CP problem
- **[Primordial black holes](../concepts/primordial-black-holes.md)** — early-universe black holes that could constitute some fraction of dark matter; the [KM3NET monster neutrino](../experiments/km3net-neutrino-detection.md) may be indirect evidence via [Hawking radiation](../concepts/hawking-radiation.md)
- **Dark sector particles** — an entire hidden ecosystem of dark particles, mirroring the different types of ordinary matter

Dark matter could behave like a particle or like a wave (if ultra-light), and may be just one member of a broader [beyond Standard Model](../open-questions/beyond-standard-model.md) sector.

## Detection Strategies

Scientists worldwide pursue four complementary approaches:

### 1. Indirect Detection
Experiments monitor energy signatures released when dark matter interacts with or annihilates other particles — on Earth or in space. Signals could include anomalous gamma rays, cosmic rays, or neutrinos from galactic centers or clusters.

### 2. Direct Detection
Underground experiments wait for a dark matter particle to interact with the detector itself — typically a heavy nucleus. The signal would be a tiny nuclear recoil. Deep underground placement reduces background cosmic-ray noise. No confirmed detection to date.

### 3. Accelerator-Based Experiments
At colliders like the [Large Hadron Collider](../experiments/large-hadron-collider.md), experiments attempt to produce dark matter particles from the energy of particle collisions. The signature would be missing transverse momentum — energy that disappears into undetected particles. The LHC has found no dark matter candidates so far, motivating [future colliders](../experiments/future-colliders.md) such as the muon collider.

### 4. Self-Interaction Searches
By examining the density profiles and internal structure of galaxies and galaxy clusters, astronomers search for signatures of dark matter self-interactions. Standard WIMP dark matter predicts certain density profiles; deviations could reveal new forces within the dark sector. This approach bridges astronomical observation and fundamental particle physics.

## Global Collaboration

Dark matter research is inherently global: experiments span underground laboratories (Gran Sasso, SNOLAB, Sanford Underground Research Facility), space telescopes, and particle accelerators across continents. Scientists share datasets internationally to accelerate progress.

## Status

Despite decades of searches, dark matter has not been directly detected. This null result — particularly from the LHC and direct-detection experiments — has deepened the [beyond Standard Model](../open-questions/beyond-standard-model.md) puzzle and motivated broader candidate classes beyond WIMPs.

## Rubin Observatory as a Dark Matter Probe

The [Vera C. Rubin Observatory](../experiments/vera-c-rubin-observatory.md) is specifically designed to investigate dark matter. Its 10-year Legacy Survey of Space and Time will map billions of galaxies, enabling statistical analyses of gravitational lensing and large-scale structure — probing how dark matter shapes the cosmic web. The observatory is named for Vera C. Rubin, who found conclusive evidence of dark matter through galaxy rotation curves.
