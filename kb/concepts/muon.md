# Muon

The muon is a fundamental particle — a heavier, shorter-lived cousin of the electron. It carries the same negative electric charge and belongs to the lepton family, but is approximately 207 times more massive and decays after just **2.2 microseconds**. Every minute, cosmic-ray muons rain through everything on Earth, penetrating meters of rock before decaying.

## Discovery

In the mid-1930s, two groups of physicists independently detected muons in cosmic ray tracks. At the time, only a handful of particles were known — electrons, protons, and photons. The muon was an entirely unexpected newcomer. Nobel laureate Isidor Isaac Rabi captured the community's bewilderment: **"Who ordered that?"**

The muon's existence forced physicists to revise their assumption that they had already identified nature's fundamental constituents. As [David Kaiser](../people/david-kaiser.md) (MIT) notes: "This was an era where physicists thought they had the parts needed to explain what the world was made of. It took a long time for experimentalists and theorists to agree that there really was a need for a new particle."

The discovery triggered a cascade — physicists now expected more unknown particles — eventually leading to the full particle zoo codified in the [Standard Model](../theories/standard-model.md).

## Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Mass | ~105.7 MeV/c² (~207 × electron mass) |
| Lifetime | 2.2 microseconds |
| Charge | −1 (elementary) |
| Spin | 1/2 (fermion) |
| Type | Elementary lepton |

## Role in Fundamental Physics

Physicists have studied muons carefully searching for deviations from [Standard Model](../theories/standard-model.md) predictions. Any anomaly would signal [physics beyond the Standard Model](../open-questions/beyond-standard-model.md). The muon's properties — fundamental yet massive — also make it the preferred candidate for a future high-energy collider (see [Future Colliders](../experiments/future-colliders.md)).

### The Anomalous Magnetic Moment (g-2)

The muon's most powerful role in fundamental physics is as a probe of the quantum vacuum. Its spin creates a magnetic moment; quantum field theory predicts a tiny deviation from the classical Dirac value (g = 2) due to virtual particle corrections from every sector of the Standard Model. This **anomalous magnetic moment** aμ = (g−2)/2 serves as a precision test: any discrepancy between experiment and theory would signal new physics.

The [Muon g-2 experiment](../experiments/muon-g2.md) at Fermilab delivered its final result in June 2025 — a measurement of **127 parts-per-billion** precision:

> aμ = 0.001 165 920 705 ± 0.000 000 000 114(stat.) ± 0.000 000 000 091(syst.)

For years this result hovered ~3–4σ above the Standard Model prediction, suggesting new physics. New lattice QCD calculations (BMW) have since narrowed this gap, leaving the theoretical interpretation actively debated.

## Applications

The muon's penetrating power and electromagnetic sensitivity give it practical uses in [muography](../concepts/muography.md) — imaging the interiors of massive structures from pyramids to volcanoes. The same detector technology developed for particle physics is repurposed for these applications.
