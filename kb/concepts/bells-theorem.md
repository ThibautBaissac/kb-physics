# Bell's Theorem

Bell's theorem, derived by [John Bell](../people/john-bell.md) in 1964, is a mathematical result showing that no theory of local hidden variables can reproduce all the statistical predictions of quantum mechanics. Experiments have confirmed quantum mechanics' predictions, ruling out local realistic descriptions of [quantum entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md).

## Background: Einstein's Challenge

Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (1935) argued that quantum mechanics was incomplete. If two entangled particles are separated and measured, quantum mechanics predicts correlated outcomes — but provides no mechanism. Einstein proposed that hidden variables must predetermine the outcomes: each particle carries a "instruction set" fixed at creation. This would restore local realism — no spooky action at a distance.

## Bell's Inequality

Bell showed that any local hidden-variable theory must satisfy a mathematical inequality on the correlations between measurements on separated particles. Quantum mechanics predicts correlations that violate this inequality. The two frameworks make different experimental predictions — so it is a testable question.

## Experimental Confirmation

Experiments beginning with Clauser et al. (1969) and Aspect (1981), and culminating in loophole-free tests (Hensen et al. 2015, Giustina et al. 2015), consistently violate Bell's inequality in the manner predicted by quantum mechanics. Local hidden-variable theories are experimentally ruled out.

## Implications

Bell's result forces a stark choice among interpretations of [quantum mechanics](../open-questions/measurement-problem.md):

1. **No pre-existing properties:** Quantum systems genuinely lack definite values before measurement (quantum indeterminism). Outcomes are created by measurement, not revealed.
2. **Nonlocality:** Measurements on separated particles instantaneously influence each other. This is in tension with special relativity, though quantum nonlocality cannot be used to transmit information faster than light.

Any satisfactory interpretation must accommodate Bell nonlocality — either by accepting (1), (2), or both.

## Bell Tests and Complex Numbers

In 2021, [Nicolas Gisin](../people/nicolas-gisin.md) extended Bell-style tests to three participants, finding correlations that appeared to demand [complex numbers](../concepts/complex-numbers-in-quantum-mechanics.md) rather than real-valued theories. However, 2025 work showed that the test assumed a specific tensor product structure; sufficiently general real-valued theories match all complex predictions.
