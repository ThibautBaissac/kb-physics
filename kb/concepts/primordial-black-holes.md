# Primordial Black Holes

Primordial black holes (PBHs) are theoretical objects that could have formed during the universe's first moments, when density spikes during cosmic expansion collapsed into black holes of varying masses. The concept was proposed by Yakov Zel'dovich and Igor Novikov (1966) and later developed by Stephen Hawking and Bernard Carr in the 1970s.

Unlike stellar black holes (formed from collapsing stars), PBHs could have been as small as atomic nuclei. As [David Kaiser](../people/david-kaiser.md) (MIT) explains: "They could form before there were even atoms, let alone stars."

## Mass Windows

Scientists have constrained the viable PBH mass range:

- **Too small** (~below 100 trillion grams): Would have already evaporated via [Hawking radiation](../concepts/hawking-radiation.md)
- **Too large** (~above lunar mass): Would have created observable gravitational effects on distant starlight
- **Viable window:** ~100 quadrillion grams (asteroid mass) to ~100 sextillion grams (lunar mass)
- **Evaporating now:** PBHs around 100 trillion grams would be finishing their evaporation today

## Dark Matter Candidate

PBHs within the viable mass windows could constitute dark matter. Wenzer Qin (NYU): "They're one of the few good theories for what dark matter could be." However, incorporating PBHs into early-universe models requires fine-tuning: "You have to tune the knob just right."

Priyamvada Natarajan (Yale) argues PBHs likely represent only a tiny fraction of dark matter but force reconsideration of alternatives beyond WIMPs and axions.

## Final Evaporation Burst

A PBH completing its evaporation produces a dramatic final explosion, releasing all 17 fundamental particles of the [Standard Model](../theories/standard-model.md) — "neutrinos and quarks and all kinds of exotic things." This burst is the basis for the hypothesis that the [KM3NET monster neutrino](../experiments/km3net-neutrino-detection.md) originated from an exploding PBH.

## Detection Prospects

- **Neutrino telescopes:** The KM3NET detection may already be evidence (see [KM3NET neutrino detection](../experiments/km3net-neutrino-detection.md))
- **Gravitational-wave detectors:** Could identify mergers of sub-stellar-mass PBHs
- **Mars orbital wobbles:** Kaiser proposes spacecraft around Mars could detect gravitational perturbations from nearby PBHs
- **Exotic particle searches:** Dark photons and dark electrons could slow Hawking radiation, meaning more PBHs may be evaporating today — Andrea Thamm and colleagues estimate a ">90% chance" of spotting such evaporation within ten years

## Connection to Early Supermassive Black Holes

The [James Webb Space Telescope](../experiments/james-webb-space-telescope.md) discovery of QSO1 — a 50-million-solar-mass black hole without a galaxy at 750 million years after the Big Bang — has renewed interest in PBHs as seeds for supermassive black holes. Hawking's 1971 proposal that the Big Bang created black holes from density fluctuations is one of several competing explanations for QSO1's existence — see [Supermassive Black Hole Origins](../open-questions/supermassive-black-hole-origins.md).

[Priyamvada Natarajan](../people/priyamvada-natarajan.md) (Yale) argues PBHs likely represent only a tiny fraction of dark matter but acknowledges their potential role in seeding early cosmic structures.
