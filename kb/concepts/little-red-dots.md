# Little Red Dots

Little Red Dots (LRDs) are a category of compact, highly luminous objects discovered by the [James Webb Space Telescope](../experiments/james-webb-space-telescope.md) in the early universe. Their name comes from their appearance in deep-field images: small, reddish pinpoints scattered across the field like freckles — the premise of the Symmetry Magazine description "the freckled universe."

Over 340 have been catalogued. They represent one of the most puzzling discoveries of the JWST era.

## Observed Properties

LRDs are defined by an unusual combination of characteristics:

- **Extreme compactness**: Radii ≤ 500 light-years — approximately 1% of the Milky Way's diameter
- **High luminosity**: Estimated to require ~100 billion stars packed into a hundredth of typical galactic space to explain their brightness through starlight alone
- **Distinctive spectral shape**: A V-shaped signature across ultraviolet and optical wavelengths, with a steep red optical slope
- **Early-universe confinement**: They appear at redshifts z ≈ 4.2–5.5, roughly 600 million to 1.5 billion years after the Big Bang, and are absent from the local universe

## Two Competing Interpretations

### Active Galactic Nuclei (Accreting Black Holes)

The leading hypothesis is that LRDs are actively accreting supermassive black holes — a novel AGN population at cosmic dawn. The RUBIES survey (Red Unknowns: Bright Infrared Extragalactic Survey) found that over 70% of examined LRDs display rapidly rotating gas, a kinematic signature associated with infalling material around a black hole. [Anthony Taylor](../people/anthony-taylor.md) (UT Austin) led this kinematic analysis (January 2025, *The Astrophysical Journal*).

If correct, these black holes are extreme: their masses represent 10–50% of their host galaxy mass, compared to the ~0.1% ratio seen in local galaxies. This would imply that black hole growth in the early universe massively outpaced star formation — reversing the co-evolutionary sequence observed today.

The central mystery under this interpretation is the **missing X-ray emission**. Standard AGN theory predicts that accreting black holes should be bright X-ray sources. LRDs are essentially X-ray silent. Proposals invoking extreme dust obscuration face the difficulty that the required dust quantities appear physically implausible at these redshifts.

### Ultra-Dense Stellar Systems

An alternative theory (arXiv:2503.16596) proposes that LRDs are "black hole stars" — extraordinarily dense concentrations of ancient stars, without an AGN. [Hollis Akins](../people/hollis-akins.md) (UT Austin) has investigated extreme stellar density models. Under this interpretation, the red V-shape arises from stellar light and dust, not AGN activity. The challenge: required stellar densities would exceed anything known in the local universe by orders of magnitude.

## Open Questions

1. **What powers their luminosity?** Black hole accretion, extreme stellar density, or something else entirely?
2. **Why no X-rays?** The missing high-energy emission is the hardest feature to explain under either model.
3. **Why do they disappear?** LRDs vanish entirely after ~1.5 billion years — what quenches them?
4. **Are they ancestors of modern supermassive black holes?** Could today's galactic-center black holes (including Sgr A*) descend from LRDs?
5. **Do they flip the black hole–galaxy formation sequence?** If black holes preceded galaxies in LRDs, co-evolutionary models need revision.

[Jenny Greene](../people/jenny-greene.md) (Princeton): "Every time we think we understand something [about LRDs], they surprise us again by not having the properties we expect."
