# Cosmological Constant

The cosmological constant (Lambda) is a term in Einstein's field equations of general relativity representing a uniform energy density of empty space. Originally introduced by Einstein to allow for a static universe, it was later reinterpreted as the energy of the vacuum — now identified with dark energy.

## Role in Spacetime Geometry

Willem de Sitter showed in 1916-17 that the sign of the cosmological constant determines the geometry of an empty universe:

- **Positive (Lambda > 0):** Spacetime is positively curved — [de Sitter space](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md). Space expands exponentially.
- **Negative (Lambda < 0):** Spacetime is negatively curved — [anti-de Sitter space](../concepts/anti-de-sitter-space.md). Space contracts, acting like a bounded box.
- **Zero (Lambda = 0):** Spacetime is flat Minkowski space.

Einstein originally believed the universe should be eternal and unchanging, requiring forces to exactly cancel out. De Sitter recognized that evolution was a natural consequence of relativity.

## The Vacuum Energy Catastrophe

The cosmological constant is closely related to [zero-point energy](../concepts/zero-point-energy.md). Quantum field theory predicts an enormous (naively infinite) vacuum energy from the ground-state fluctuations of all fields. Since gravity responds to all forms of energy, this should produce a cosmological constant ~10^120 times larger than observed. As Wolfgang Pauli recognized in 1946, such energy should gravitationally explode the universe. Why the observed value is so small remains one of the deepest puzzles in physics. Sean Carroll (Johns Hopkins): "All forms of energy gravitate. That includes the vacuum energy, so you can't ignore it."

## Our Universe

Observations indicate our universe has a small, positive cosmological constant. This means the universe is expanding and will asymptotically approach [de Sitter space](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md) in the far future. The cosmological constant is associated with dark energy, which currently dominates the energy budget of the universe and drives its accelerating expansion.

## Is It Really Constant?

Until recently, observations were consistent with a fixed cosmological constant. But in April 2024, the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) presented tentative evidence that dark energy is weakening — bolstered a year later. Miguel Montero: "If those results are here to stay, they are really hinting that the cosmological constant is not a constant."

This matches the first explicit [de Sitter solution](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md) from [string theory](../theories/string-theory.md) (Bento & Montero, 2025), which naturally produces dynamical, weakening dark energy via Casimir-like effects in [compactified](../concepts/compactification.md) extra dimensions. David Andriot (CNRS) notes that dynamical dark energy "is much easier to get from string theory" than a fixed constant.
