# Supernova

A supernova is the catastrophic explosion marking the death of a massive star. Core-collapse supernovae — the most energetically extreme type — are among the most powerful events in the observable universe and are the primary astrophysical source of [neutrinos](../concepts/neutrino.md) detectable on Earth.

## Core-Collapse Mechanism

When a star with at least **8 times the Sun's mass** exhausts its nuclear fuel, it can no longer support itself against gravitational collapse. The iron core collapses in milliseconds, reaching nuclear densities. The outer layers rebound off the stiffened core and are expelled in a shockwave — the supernova.

The collapse produces:
- An intense **neutrino burst** lasting ~10 seconds
- Eventually a **neutron star** or **black hole** remnant
- A visible explosion that can outshine an entire galaxy for weeks

## The Neutrino Signal

Although supernovae appear spectacular in visible light, approximately **99% of the gravitational binding energy** (~3 × 10⁵³ ergs) is released as neutrinos and antineutrinos of all flavors. The total visible light output is a tiny fraction of this energy.

Because [neutrinos](../concepts/neutrino.md) interact so weakly with matter, they escape the stellar core immediately at the moment of collapse, while the shock wave takes hours to reach the stellar surface. This creates a crucial time delay:

> **Neutrinos arrive at Earth hours before the visible light.**

The 1987 detection of 24 neutrinos from Supernova 1987A in the Large Magellanic Cloud confirmed this prediction and inaugurated the era of neutrino astronomy.

## Neutrinos as Stellar Probes

Supernova neutrinos are "black-box recorders": they carry direct imprints of conditions inside the collapsing core — at densities (~10¹⁴ g/cm³) and temperatures (~10¹¹ K) that are impossible to recreate in any laboratory. Measuring their flavor composition, timing, and energy spectrum constrains:
- The stellar equation of state
- The neutron star formation mechanism
- Neutrino mass hierarchy
- Potential physics [beyond the Standard Model](../open-questions/beyond-standard-model.md)

## Early Warning Systems

Modern neutrino detectors such as the [DUNE Experiment](../experiments/dune-experiment.md) are designed to trigger automatically on supernova neutrino bursts and alert the global astronomy community before the explosion becomes optically visible. This gives observatories time to capture the **shock breakout** — the first flash of light escaping the star — and other early-time phenomena that encode information about the progenitor star.

A Milky Way supernova is expected every 30–50 years on average; the last galactic one visible to the naked eye was in 1604 (Kepler's supernova). The global detector network — DUNE, Super-Kamiokande, IceCube, [KM3NET](../experiments/km3net-neutrino-detection.md) — stands ready.
