# Quantum Darwinism

Quantum Darwinism is a framework developed by [Wojciech Zurek](../people/wojciech-zurek.md) (Los Alamos) that extends [decoherence](../concepts/decoherence.md) to explain how a unique classical reality emerges from quantum mechanics. It uses only the standard equations of quantum mechanics — no new assumptions.

## Pointer States

Not all quantum states are equally fragile under decoherence. Some states — called "pointer states" — can interact with the environment and generate multiple informational imprints without being destroyed. These correspond to classically observable properties like position and charge.

Pointer states survive because the interactions that create each imprint retain the quantum system in its original state, rather than knocking it into a different one. Photons, for example, can bounce off an atom and carry away positional information without altering the atom's quantum state.

## Selection and Amplification

By analogy with Darwinian natural selection, pointer states are the "fittest" quantum states: they survive the process of environmental copying. Zurek explains: "They can survive the process of copying, and so the information about them can multiply."

The amplification is enormous. Zurek and Jess Riedel calculated (2010) that within a microsecond, photons from the sun imprint the location of a dust grain about 10 million times.

## Consensus and Objectivity

A critical prediction of quantum Darwinism is that all environmental imprints of a pointer state must be identical. This means different observers examining different fragments of the environment will agree on what they observe — a unique classical world emerges necessarily.

This "consensus" replaces the mysterious and ad hoc process of [wavefunction](../concepts/wave-function.md) collapse. The observed object, surrounded by a cloud of identical imprints, forms what Zurek calls an "extanton" — an element of "relatively objective existence."

## The "Epiontic" Wave Function

Zurek claims quantum Darwinism reconciles the Copenhagen and many-worlds interpretations. Rather than the wave function being purely epistemic (describing knowledge, as in Copenhagen) or purely ontic (describing reality, as in many-worlds), Zurek argues it is "epiontic" — both at once:

- Before decoherence, all quantum possibilities exist in some sense (ontic)
- Decoherence and quantum Darwinism select one as observable reality (epistemic)
- The unselected possibilities remain in an abstract space, never growing into observable realities

## Experimental Tests

Quantum Darwinism makes testable predictions. It predicts that most information about a quantum system can be gleaned from just a few environmental imprints — information content "saturates" quickly. Preliminary experiments confirm this, with more rigorous tests underway.
