# Photon

The photon is the elementary particle of the electromagnetic field and the force carrier of the electromagnetic interaction in the [Standard Model](../theories/standard-model.md). It is massless, has no electric charge, and travels at the cosmic speed limit — meaning, unlike massive particles, it does not experience time. Yet photons are the quanta of light through which virtually all scientific observation is conducted: from Galileo's telescope to the [James Webb Space Telescope](../experiments/james-webb-space-telescope.md).

[Claudio Pellegrini](../people/claudio-pellegrini.md) (UCLA): "Photons have always been key to how we observe and comprehend nature."

## Historical Understanding

The nature of light was debated for centuries:

- **1675 — Newton's corpuscles**: Isaac Newton proposed light consists of tiny particles ("corpuscles"), but struggled to explain how it propagated through the apparent void of space.
- **19th century — wave theory and ether**: Scientists favored a universal medium (the "ether") through which light waves would propagate. James Clerk Maxwell's equations showed that wavelength determines position in the electromagnetic spectrum — from radio waves to gamma rays.
- **1900 — Planck's quanta**: Max Planck theorized that light energy was exchanged in discrete packets, though he initially treated this as a mathematical device.
- **1905 — Einstein's photon**: Albert Einstein proposed that light itself *is* composed of discrete energy packets — what we now call photons. This explanation of the photoelectric effect earned him the 1921 Nobel Prize.
- **Compton scattering**: Arthur Compton's experiments confirmed that photons behave as particles with definite momentum, consistent with wave-particle duality.
- **de Broglie extension**: Louis de Broglie extended wave-particle duality to all matter, establishing it as a universal quantum feature — not a curiosity of light alone.

## Wave-Particle Duality

Photons exhibit both particle and wave properties depending on the measurement context. This is not a paradox but a feature of [quantum mechanics](../theories/quantum-field-theory.md): the [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) of a photon gives the probability amplitude for detection at any point in space. Contemporary physicists treat the duality as a flexible conceptual tool, adopting whichever picture is more useful for a given experiment.

## Quantum Properties

Photons carry polarization — the orientation of their electromagnetic oscillation. Polarization states can encode quantum information, and photons can become [entangled](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md), sharing a joint quantum state regardless of separation. Experiments demonstrating photon entanglement from the 1970s through the 1990s — culminating in Bell test violations — catalyzed the quantum information science revolution and eventually contributed to quantum computing and cryptography.

Pellegrini: "Photons have demonstrated quantum mechanics's strangest characteristics."

## Applications

The practical mastery of photons underlies much of modern science and technology:

- **Lasers, microscopes, telescopes**: Controlled photon emission and detection tools
- **Synchrotron X-rays**: Intense X-ray beams at facilities like [SLAC's light sources](../experiments/slac-light-sources.md) resolve cellular and molecular structures, and have even recovered ancient manuscripts
- **Infrared astronomy**: The JWST collects infrared photons from over 13.5 billion years ago, probing the first galaxies and black holes
- **Quantum computers**: Photonic qubits exploit polarization states and entanglement for quantum information processing
