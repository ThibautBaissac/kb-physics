# Contextuality

Contextuality is a fundamental feature of quantum mechanics in which the outcome of measuring a property depends on what other properties are being measured at the same time. It is a lesser-known but deeply important aspect of quantum theory, distinct from [entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md).

## Classical vs. Quantum

In classical physics, a property has a definite value regardless of what else is measured alongside it. In quantum mechanics, certain properties cannot be assigned pre-existing values that are independent of the measurement context. The result you get for one observable can change depending on which other compatible observables you choose to measure simultaneously.

## Connection to Quantum Advantage

Contextuality is the crucial ingredient that distinguishes [magic states](../concepts/magic-states.md) from stabilizer states. The Gottesman-Knill theorem showed that highly entangled stabilizer states — which are noncontextual — can be efficiently simulated classically. Magic states, which require contextuality, cannot. This was identified in 2014 as the key resource enabling genuine quantum computational advantage.

## Detection at the LHC

The CMS experiment at the [Large Hadron Collider](../experiments/large-hadron-collider.md) detected contextuality indirectly by measuring magic in entangled top quark pairs. The spin correlation matrix of top-antitop pairs, measured across x, y, and z directions, confirmed the presence of magic — and therefore contextuality — in the quantum states produced by proton-proton collisions.
