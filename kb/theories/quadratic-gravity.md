# Quadratic Gravity

Quadratic gravity is the unique [renormalizable](../concepts/renormalization.md) [quantum field theory](../theories/quantum-field-theory.md) of gravity. Where Einstein's general relativity has a single curvature term, quadratic gravity adds two curvature-squared terms, yielding a theory that can be consistently quantized using standard QFT techniques — just like electromagnetism.

## Origin (1977)

[Kellogg Stelle](../people/kellogg-stelle.md), then a graduate student at Brandeis University, discovered that adding curvature-squared terms to Einstein's equation was the only way to make gravity [renormalizable](../concepts/renormalization.md). The modified equation allows spacetime ripples to become less significant at smaller scales, so their net effects can be captured in a few measurable constants — analogous to the electron's charge and mass in electromagnetism.

The enhanced spacetime fabric hosts three types of particle-ripples:
1. **Normal gravitons** — from the original Einstein curvature term
2. **A scalar particle** — Stelle's "sweet little scalar," inoffensive
3. **A ghost particle** — a negative-energy particle that threatened to destabilize the theory

## The Ghost Problem

The third particle carries a minus sign that produces two pathological effects: negative energy (meaning spacetime gains energy by creating ghosts, leading to runaway particle production) and negative probabilities (a meaningless proposition). Physicists called such theories "sick" — mathematically inconsistent. See [ghost particles](../concepts/ghost-particles.md).

After Stelle's 1977 paper, physicists quickly moved on to supergravity (which posited superpartner particles) and then [string theory](../theories/string-theory.md). Quadratic gravity languished with only 10–20 citations per year.

## Revival (2010s–Present)

Interest renewed as [string theory](../theories/string-theory.md) failed to deliver promised breakthroughs and superpartners failed to materialize at the [LHC](../experiments/large-hadron-collider.md).

### Hierarchy Problem Connection (2014)

Alberto Salvio and Alessandro Strumia found that quadratic gravity's two extra particles could help explain the [hierarchy problem](../concepts/hierarchy-problem.md) — why gravity is so much weaker than the other forces. This prompted fresh scrutiny of whether ghosts were truly fatal.

### Taming the Ghosts

Multiple researchers found overlapping solutions to the ghost problem:

- **Damiano Anselmi** (Pisa) discovered alternative versions of Feynman's rules that avoid ghost pitfalls entirely
- **[John Donoghue](../people/john-donoghue.md)** and Gabriel Menezes showed that in simple scenarios, ghost particles vanish before wreaking havoc — the vacuum stays calm and probabilities remain positive (unitarity preserved)
- **Salvio and Bob Holdom** independently found ways to tweak probability calculations to guarantee positive values
- Donoghue proposed that ghosts' main cost is temporary violations of causality — ghost particles can briefly skip backward in time, with the forward flow of time emerging as an average over "temporally squishy micro-moments"

[Luca Buoninfante](../people/luca-buoninfante.md) (Radboud University): "Mathematically, they make sense now."

Skeptics remain. Alessia Platania (Copenhagen): "They're working hard. But I would say it's still open as a question."

## Asymptotic Freedom

Donoghue and collaborators discovered that quadratic gravity is [asymptotically free](../concepts/asymptotic-freedom.md) — as graviton collisions become more intense, gravity gets weaker, making calculations easier. This property (shared by the strong nuclear force) suggests that quadratic gravity never breaks down and could describe physics at arbitrarily small scales. "This could allow it to be the final theory," Donoghue said — though he added, "I'm not convinced that this is the final theory."

## Starobinsky Inflation

Stelle's scalar particle is exactly the kind of field that could drive [cosmic inflation](../concepts/cosmic-inflation.md). The Soviet physicist Alexei Starobinsky used quadratic gravity in 1980 to formulate the first theory of inflation. Recent work suggests Starobinsky inflation produces gravitational waves too small for current telescopes but potentially detectable by next-generation observatories. Donoghue: "Starobinsky inflation is the only remaining one that makes sense from a quantum field theory point of view."

## Implications for Reality

If quadratic gravity is correct, spacetime could be a true continuum at all scales — no strings, loops, or other exotic substructure. Bob Holdom: "We're talking about a true continuum description all the way to arbitrarily small scales. Space-time forever."

Alternatively, quadratic gravity may be a "closed, self-consistent layer of reality" — an effective theory that works without needing to resolve what happens at the very smallest scales. Stelle called it "a possible link, a kind of intermediate regime."
