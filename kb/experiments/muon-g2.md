# Muon g-2 Experiment

The Muon g-2 experiment (pronounced "g minus two") at Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory measures the anomalous magnetic moment of the [muon](../concepts/muon.md) — one of the most precise measurements ever made in particle physics and a sensitive test of the [Standard Model](../theories/standard-model.md).

## What It Measures

A muon's spin creates a tiny magnetic moment, described by the Dirac equation as g = 2 exactly. Quantum field theory predicts a small deviation from this value due to contributions from virtual particles — called the **anomalous magnetic moment** aμ = (g−2)/2. Because virtual particles from *all* sectors of the Standard Model (and potentially beyond it) contribute to this correction, aμ is an extraordinarily sensitive probe of new physics.

## Final Result (2025)

The experiment's third and final measurement, combining six years of data (2018–2023), achieved a precision of **127 parts-per-billion** — exceeding the original design goal of 140 ppb:

> **aμ = 0.001 165 920 705 ± 0.000 000 000 114(stat.) ± 0.000 000 000 091(syst.)**

This result agrees with the 2021 and 2023 published results but with substantially improved precision. Physicist Peter Winter: "We not only achieved our goals but exceeded them, which is not very easy for these precision measurements."

## The Discrepancy Question

For years, the Muon g-2 experimental world average sat approximately **3–4σ above** the Standard Model prediction, generating excitement about possible [physics beyond the Standard Model](../open-questions/beyond-standard-model.md). However, new theoretical calculations using lattice [quantum chromodynamics](../concepts/quantum-chromodynamics.md) — the BMW calculation — have brought the theoretical prediction closer to the experimental value. The status of the discrepancy is now under active debate: if the BMW calculation is correct, the tension is reduced; if earlier data-driven hadronic vacuum polarization estimates are used, tension persists.

## Experimental Technique

The experiment stores muons in a 14-meter-diameter storage ring under a precisely known magnetic field. As muons circulate, their spins precess at a rate that encodes aμ. The anomalous precession frequency — the difference between spin precession and cyclotron frequency — is measured to extract aμ.

The collaboration is uniquely multidisciplinary: 176 scientists from 34 institutions across 7 countries, combining expertise from high-energy, accelerator, atomic, and nuclear physics.

## Historical Context

The study of the electron's g-factor stretches back nearly a century. Cornell professor Lawrence Gibbons noted that "g-2 has been teaching us a lot about the nature of nature" over that time. Muon g-2 pushes this lineage into the precision frontier, using the muon's greater mass to amplify sensitivity to heavy virtual particles that would contribute to BSM physics.
