# Gravitational Wave Detectors

Gravitational wave detectors are laser interferometers that measure the stretching and squeezing of spacetime caused by passing gravitational waves — ripples in the fabric of spacetime generated when massive objects accelerate, particularly when black holes or neutron stars merge.

## Current Detector Network

### LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory)
- **Location**: Two facilities in the United States (Hanford, WA and Livingston, LA)
- **First detection**: September 14, 2015 — two merging black holes, ~1.3 billion light-years away
- **Significance**: First direct detection of gravitational waves, confirming a key prediction of general relativity

### Virgo
- **Location**: Cascina, Italy (European detector)
- **First joint detection with LIGO**: August 2017
- **Role**: Provides a third detector baseline, enabling sky localization of sources

### KAGRA
- **Location**: Kamioka, Japan (underground detector)
- **Status**: Joined the network after LIGO and Virgo
- **Novel design**: Underground location reduces seismic noise; uses cryogenic mirrors

Together, these three detectors have recorded hundreds of gravitational wave signals over approximately a decade.

## What Gravitational Waves Reveal About Black Holes

When two black holes merge, the event produces three phases of gravitational wave emission:

1. **Inspiral**: The two black holes spiral together, emitting increasingly strong waves
2. **Merger**: The two horizons combine in a fraction of a millisecond
3. **Ringdown**: The merged black hole oscillates and settles into its final Kerr state, emitting damped gravitational waves

The **ringdown** is particularly valuable: its frequency and decay rate are determined by the black hole's mass and spin alone — if the [no-hair theorem](../concepts/no-hair-theorem.md) holds. Any deviation in the ringdown from GR predictions would indicate structure near the horizon — "hair."

## Constraining the No-Hair Conjecture

Vitor Cardoso (Niels Bohr Institute), Gregorio Carullo (University of Birmingham), Simon Maenaut (KU Leuven), and colleagues analyzed 22 black hole merger events (arXiv:2411.17893, 2025). A 2023 KU Leuven breakthrough (arXiv:2304.02663) enabled analysis of fast-spinning black holes under modified gravity theories. Result:

**Any deviation from general relativity must be confined to within 40 kilometers of the event horizon (95% confidence).**

This is among the most precise tests of GR in the strong-field regime ever performed.

## Next-Generation Detectors

The current 40 km constraint will improve dramatically with planned facilities:

| Detector | Location | Timeline | Key Capability |
|----------|----------|----------|----------------|
| **Einstein Telescope** | Europe (underground) | 2030s | Full-sky, all black holes in mass range |
| **Cosmic Explorer** | United States | 2030s | 10× LIGO sensitivity |
| **Indian LIGO** | India | ~2030 | Improved sky localization |

The Einstein Telescope will be able to detect deviations at the scale of a football field's length — approaching the scale of quantum gravity effects at stellar-mass black hole horizons.

Gregorio Carullo: "The Einstein Telescope will basically observe all the black holes in the universe of a certain mass range … which is incredible."
