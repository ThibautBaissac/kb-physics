# Electron Fluid Experiments

A series of experiments from 2017 to 2025 confirmed the theoretical prediction (Gurzhi, 1963) that electrons can be made to flow like a fluid when momentum is conserved.

## Key Milestones

### Gurzhi Effect in Graphene (2017)

Andre Geim and collaborators carved a choke point into a strip of [graphene](../concepts/graphene.md), sent electrons through, and measured resistance as a function of temperature. As temperature increased, resistance *decreased* — the opposite of normal metals. This was the Gurzhi effect: direct evidence that electrons were in the [hydrodynamic regime](../concepts/electron-hydrodynamics.md), where heat reduces viscosity rather than increasing scattering.

### Electron Whirlpools (2022, Weizmann Institute)

Physicists at the Weizmann Institute of Science shaped tungsten diselenide into a wire with two circular side chambers ("Mickey Mouse ears"). By measuring the magnetic fields generated by electron motion, they directly observed electrons swirling backward into the chambers — forming whirlpools analogous to eddies where a river's current meets a bend. [Thomas Scaffidi](../people/thomas-scaffidi.md) (UC Irvine): "They can really see these vortices."

### Supersonic Electron Shock Waves (2025, Columbia)

Johannes Geurs, a postdoc in [Cory Dean](../people/cory-dean.md)'s lab at Columbia, pushed electron fluids "to the extreme." He carved bilayer graphene into a de Laval nozzle shape (the same geometry used in rocket engines) to accelerate electrons beyond the "speed of sound" of the electron fluid — a few hundred kilometers per second.

When the accelerated electrons crashed into slower electrons downstream, the slower electrons couldn't move out of the way fast enough, creating a shock wave — the electron equivalent of a sonic boom. The researchers detected the pileup by sweeping a metallic tip over the sample and measuring changes in the electric field.

Scaffidi called this "really the frontier right now."

## Significance

These experiments demonstrate progressively dramatic fluid behavior in electrons, opening two frontiers:

1. **Device engineering:** Electron fluid behavior depends on channel geometry, enabling new types of electronic components.
2. **Theoretical tools:** Hydrodynamic descriptions (density, viscosity, conservation laws) could provide simplified frameworks for understanding complex quantum materials without tracking individual electrons.
