# James Webb Space Telescope

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is NASA's flagship infrared space observatory, launched in December 2021. Its unprecedented sensitivity in the infrared has opened a new window into the early universe, revealing objects from the first billion years of cosmic history.

## Early-Universe Black Holes

JWST has identified over 300 "little red dots" — compact objects in the early universe that exhibit characteristics of actively accreting (glowing) black holes. These objects appear unusual compared to known galaxy-black hole systems, creating confusion about their true nature.

The most striking discovery is QSO1, a 50-million-solar-mass black hole existing without a surrounding galaxy when the universe was just 750 million years old. Identified by [Lukas Furtak](../people/lukas-furtak.md) (Ben-Gurion University) through gravitational lens imagery in 2023, QSO1 appeared as three tiny red dots that showed no signs of distortion despite lensing — distinguishing them from galaxies.

[Roberto Maiolino](../people/roberto-maiolino.md) (Cambridge) led follow-up observations in 2024, achieving pixel-level spectral analysis that confirmed the mass and revealed no parent galaxy. The orbiting gas is essentially pure hydrogen — primordial material unchanged since the Big Bang — suggesting the black hole formed before nearby stars had enriched the region with heavier elements.

Dale Kocevski (Colby College): "Very rarely do you find things you can't explain."

## Implications for Cosmic History

These discoveries challenge the conventional model where stars collapse and merge over billions of years to form supermassive black holes. The existence of massive black holes within the universe's first billion years suggests alternative formation mechanisms — see [Supermassive Black Hole Origins](../open-questions/supermassive-black-hole-origins.md).

John Regan (Maynooth University) notes these objects "tell us we don't know anything" about early cosmic history, though he finds this "really exciting and very electrifying for the field."
