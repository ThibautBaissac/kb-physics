# DUNE Experiment

The Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE) is a flagship particle physics experiment located at the Sanford Underground Research Facility (SURF) in Lead, South Dakota. It is designed to serve a dual purpose: studying [neutrino](../concepts/neutrino.md) properties and acting as an early-warning system for galactic [supernovae](../concepts/supernova.md).

## Design and Location

DUNE uses massive liquid argon time-projection chamber (LArTPC) detectors placed deep underground to shield them from cosmic ray backgrounds. The underground location — like the [KM3NET](../experiments/km3net-neutrino-detection.md) detector — exploits the fact that neutrinos pass through rock effortlessly while cosmic-ray backgrounds are filtered out.

## Supernova Watch

When a massive star (≥8 solar masses) undergoes [core-collapse](../concepts/supernova.md), it releases ~99% of its energy as a burst of neutrinos and antineutrinos in the first few seconds. Because neutrinos interact so rarely with matter, they escape the collapsing core immediately — arriving at Earth **hours before the visible light**.

DUNE's detectors run continuously, and researchers have developed **trigger algorithms** to rapidly identify supernova neutrino bursts among the millions of routine interactions recorded per day. Upon a detection, automated alerts will be distributed globally, giving optical and radio astronomers time to orient their telescopes before the explosion becomes visible.

## Key Advantage: Detecting Both Neutrinos and Antineutrinos

A crucial distinguishing feature of DUNE is its ability to detect **both neutrinos and antineutrinos**. Earlier experiments — such as the Kamiokande detector that captured 11 neutrinos from Supernova 1987A — could only detect electron antineutrinos via inverse beta decay. DUNE's argon-based detection mechanism is sensitive to electron neutrinos via charged-current interactions, complementing antineutrino-sensitive water-Cherenkov detectors.

This dual sensitivity enables:
- **Matter-antimatter comparisons** within the supernova neutrino flux
- Better constraints on the neutrino mass hierarchy
- Probes of the stellar equation of state under extreme densities

## Neutrinos as "Black-Box Recorders"

Scientists describe supernova neutrinos as "black-box recorders" — they carry direct information about the physics inside the collapsing stellar core, at densities and temperatures impossible to recreate in any terrestrial laboratory. The [Standard Model](../theories/standard-model.md) predicts neutrino flavors and mixing, but extreme supernova environments may reveal deviations that point toward [physics beyond the Standard Model](../open-questions/beyond-standard-model.md).

## Context

DUNE joins a growing global network of neutrino detectors on alert for the next galactic supernova, including Super-Kamiokande (Japan) and IceCube (Antarctica). The last confirmed galactic supernova neutrino detection was Supernova 1987A in the Large Magellanic Cloud. A supernova in the Milky Way — expected every 30–50 years on average — would produce thousands of detectable neutrinos across the global network.
