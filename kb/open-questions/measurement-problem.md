# Measurement Problem

The measurement problem is the central interpretive puzzle of quantum mechanics: how and why does a quantum system in superposition — with multiple possible outcomes — produce a single definite result when measured? More broadly, how does the classical world we experience emerge from quantum rules?

## The Problem

In quantum mechanics, a system's [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) encodes all possible measurement outcomes as a superposition. Measurement seemingly "collapses" the superposition into a single value. But the theory provides no mechanism for this collapse — it only predicts the *probabilities* of outcomes, not which specific outcome will occur.

As Jeffrey Bub (University of Maryland) puts it, quantum uncertainty is "a new sort of ignorance about something that doesn't yet have a truth value, something that simply isn't one way or the other before we measure."

Heisenberg proposed a "cut" dividing quantum and classical physics, but modern experiments show quantum behavior in objects visible under optical microscopes, making any sharp boundary untenable.

## Major Interpretations

| Interpretation | Position on Collapse | Key Objection |
|----------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Copenhagen** (Bohr, Heisenberg) | Collapse is figurative — reflects classical experience | Relies on an arbitrary quantum/classical cut |
| **Spontaneous collapse** (GRW) | Collapse is a real, random physical event | Mechanism unclear |
| **Pilot wave** (de Broglie, Bohm) | No collapse — particles have definite properties guided by a wave | Requires a mysterious guiding field |
| **Many worlds** (Everett) | No collapse — all outcomes realized in branching universes | Requires wild proliferation of unobservable universes |

## Zurek's Resolution: Decoherence + Quantum Darwinism

[Wojciech Zurek](../people/wojciech-zurek.md) argues that the measurement problem can be dissolved — not solved by adding new physics, but resolved using only the standard equations of quantum mechanics:

1. **[Decoherence](../concepts/decoherence.md):** [Entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) with the environment destroys superpositions almost instantly at macroscopic scales, explaining why we don't observe them.

2. **[Quantum Darwinism](../concepts/quantum-darwinism.md):** Environmentally robust "pointer states" generate multiple identical imprints in the environment. All observers examining different environmental fragments see the same outcome — a unique classical world emerges necessarily.

3. **Epiontic wave function:** The wave function is both epistemic and ontic. Before decoherence, all possibilities exist; decoherence selects one as observable reality. The rest remain in an abstract space, never becoming observable.

## Remaining Questions

Even under Zurek's framework, open issues persist:
- **Why this outcome?** What selects a particular result from the probability distribution? Must we accept fundamental randomness?
- **Irreversibility threshold:** At what point does a quantum system irrevocably commit to a particular outcome?
- **Observer disagreement:** [Renato Renner](../people/renato-renner.md) (ETH Zurich) has constructed scenarios where different observers cannot agree on outcomes, suggesting no interpretation is fully satisfactory yet.
- **The quantum substrate:** [Sally Shrapnel](../people/sally-shrapnel.md) (Queensland) notes the framework doesn't explain what the pre-decoherence quantum domain actually *is*.

## Connection to De Sitter Space

The measurement problem shares a deep theme with [quantum gravity in de Sitter space](../open-questions/quantum-gravity-in-de-sitter-space.md): both involve the role of the observer. In de Sitter space, there is no external vantage point from which to make measurements — the observer is always inside the quantum system. This mirrors the measurement problem's challenge of separating observer from system.
