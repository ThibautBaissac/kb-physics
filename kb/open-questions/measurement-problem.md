# Measurement Problem

The measurement problem is the central interpretive puzzle of quantum mechanics: how and why does a quantum system in superposition — with multiple possible outcomes — produce a single definite result when measured? More broadly, how does the classical world we experience emerge from quantum rules?

## The Problem

In quantum mechanics, a system's [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) encodes all possible measurement outcomes as a superposition. Measurement seemingly "collapses" the superposition into a single value. But the theory provides no mechanism for this collapse — it only predicts the *probabilities* of outcomes, not which specific outcome will occur.

As Jeffrey Bub (University of Maryland) puts it, quantum uncertainty is "a new sort of ignorance about something that doesn't yet have a truth value, something that simply isn't one way or the other before we measure."

Heisenberg proposed a "cut" dividing quantum and classical physics, but modern experiments show quantum behavior in objects visible under optical microscopes, making any sharp boundary untenable.

## Major Interpretations

| Interpretation | Position on Collapse | Key Objection |
|----------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Copenhagen** (Bohr, [Heisenberg](../people/werner-heisenberg.md)) | Collapse is figurative — reflects classical experience | Relies on an arbitrary quantum/classical cut |
| **Spontaneous collapse** (GRW) | Collapse is a real, random physical event | Mechanism unclear |
| **Pilot wave** (de Broglie, Bohm) | No collapse — particles have definite properties guided by a wave | Requires a mysterious guiding field |
| **Many worlds** (Everett) | No collapse — all outcomes realized in branching universes | Requires wild proliferation of unobservable universes |
| **[QBism](../concepts/qbism.md)** ([Fuchs](../people/chris-fuchs.md)) | Wave function is a personal probability catalog; collapse is Bayesian updating | Seems to abandon objective physics |
| **[Relational QM](../concepts/relational-quantum-mechanics.md)** ([Rovelli](../people/carlo-rovelli.md)) | Properties exist only relative to interactions; collapse is relational fact | Different observers cannot compare notes consistently |

## Zurek's Resolution: Decoherence + Quantum Darwinism

[Wojciech Zurek](../people/wojciech-zurek.md) argues that the measurement problem can be dissolved — not solved by adding new physics, but resolved using only the standard equations of quantum mechanics:

1. **[Decoherence](../concepts/decoherence.md):** [Entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) with the environment destroys superpositions almost instantly at macroscopic scales, explaining why we don't observe them.

2. **[Quantum Darwinism](../concepts/quantum-darwinism.md):** Environmentally robust "pointer states" generate multiple identical imprints in the environment. All observers examining different environmental fragments see the same outcome — a unique classical world emerges necessarily.

3. **Epiontic wave function:** The wave function is both epistemic and ontic. Before decoherence, all possibilities exist; decoherence selects one as observable reality. The rest remain in an abstract space, never becoming observable.

## Remaining Questions

Even under Zurek's framework, open issues persist:
- **Why this outcome?** What selects a particular result from the probability distribution? Must we accept fundamental randomness?
- **Irreversibility threshold:** At what point does a quantum system irrevocably commit to a particular outcome?
- **Observer disagreement:** [Renato Renner](../people/renato-renner.md) (ETH Zurich) has constructed scenarios where different observers cannot agree on outcomes, suggesting no interpretation is fully satisfactory yet.
- **The quantum substrate:** [Sally Shrapnel](../people/sally-shrapnel.md) (Queensland) notes the framework doesn't explain what the pre-decoherence quantum domain actually *is*.

## Top Quark Decay as Measurement

Entangled top quark pairs at the [LHC](../experiments/large-hadron-collider.md) offer a new experimental window on the measurement problem. When a top quark decays, it appears to "choose" a spin direction, with decay particles traveling accordingly. [Alan Barr](../people/alan-barr.md) (Oxford) observed that "mathematically, it's an equivalent process to making a measurement" — the decay process implements a quantum measurement on the entangled system.

This raises testable questions: does [entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) persist through decay? Are the decay products of one top quark still entangled with the surviving anti-top quark? Quantum field theory predicts they should be, but no one has tested it.

However, Herbert Dreiner (University of Bonn) has raised concerns about circularity: measuring entanglement requires translating decay products' angular motion into spin states using quantum mechanical theory — potentially making it circular to use such measurements to test quantum mechanics itself.

## Connection to De Sitter Space and Closed Universes

The measurement problem shares a deep theme with [quantum gravity in de Sitter space](../open-questions/quantum-gravity-in-de-sitter-space.md): both involve the role of the observer. In de Sitter space, there is no external vantage point from which to make measurements — the observer is always inside the quantum system.

This connection became sharper with the discovery that closed universes have one-dimensional [Hilbert spaces](../concepts/hilbert-space.md) — containing zero information — unless a classical observer is present. [Ying Zhao](../people/ying-zhao.md), [Daniel Harlow](../people/daniel-harlow.md), and colleagues (MIT, 2025) showed that an observer introduces a boundary that restores complexity. If correct, this suggests that the observer/system distinction isn't just a convenience of quantum mechanics but may be structurally essential to the existence of a complex universe — inverting the traditional goal of deriving observers from objective physics.

## Emerging Research: Apparent Quantum from Classical

A striking new research direction (de les Coves and Spekkens, 2025) shows that classical universes — where particles have definite positions at all times — could nonetheless *appear* quantum to observers trapped inside them. If correct, the wave function's multiple possibilities might represent epistemic ignorance rather than ontological superposition. This would favor epistemic interpretations (Copenhagen, QBism) over ontic ones (Many-Worlds).

## Bell's Theorem as Constraint

Any interpretation must accommodate [Bell's theorem](../concepts/bells-theorem.md): [John Bell](../people/john-bell.md) proved that no local hidden-variable theory can reproduce quantum predictions, and experiments have confirmed quantum nonlocality. This rules out classical hidden-variable restorations of realism (Einstein's original hope) and sharpens the interpretive stakes: whatever quantum states describe, they cannot simply be ignorance of pre-existing local values.

## The Helgoland Centennial

The June 2025 conference on Helgoland — where [Werner Heisenberg](../people/werner-heisenberg.md) first formulated quantum mechanics in 1925 — brought ~300 physicists together to assess 100 years of progress. The verdict was striking: no consensus on interpretation has emerged. As one attendee put it, "It's just embarrassing that we don't have a story we can tell people about what reality is." Another noted it was "the first conference I've been to where people spoke openly and honestly about quantum mechanics missing something."
