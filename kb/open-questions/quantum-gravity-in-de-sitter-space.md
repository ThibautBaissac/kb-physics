# Quantum Gravity in de Sitter Space

How do we formulate a consistent quantum theory in [de Sitter space](../concepts/de-sitter-space.md) — the expanding spacetime geometry that our universe most closely resembles? This is one of the deepest open problems in theoretical physics.

## Why It's Hard

In flat space and [anti-de Sitter space](../concepts/anti-de-sitter-space.md), physicists can define quantum observables by retreating to a boundary far from quantum fluctuations. De Sitter space offers no such refuge:

1. **No accessible boundary:** Quantum fluctuations of gravity persist everywhere. [Daniel Green](../people/daniel-green.md) (UCSD): "Gravity is fluctuating, quantum mechanically, everywhere. And there's no place to shield yourself from that."

2. **Observer-system entanglement:** Quantum mechanics assumes an external observer measuring a system. In de Sitter space, the observer is always part of the quantum system — "stuck inside their own experiment."

3. **Multiple horizons:** Unlike a black hole (single horizon), de Sitter space has observer-dependent [cosmological horizons](../concepts/cosmological-horizon.md), preventing a universal boundary for [holographic](../concepts/holography.md) calculations.

4. **Energy non-conservation:** The expansion continually pumps energy into the universe. [João Penedones](../people/joao-penedones.md) (EPFL): "The expansion is literally pumping energy, changing the universe."

5. **Particle instability:** Even massless photons can be composed of massive particles in de Sitter space (Penedones & [Loparco](../people/manuel-loparco.md), 2025), implying particles can spontaneously decay and re-form.

6. **The empty Hilbert space puzzle:** When [Tom Hartman](../people/tom-hartman.md) (Stanford) applies holographic methods to de Sitter space, the result suggests it "wants to not have any states in it" — contradicting the particle-filled universe we observe. This connects directly to [Maldacena](../people/juan-maldacena.md)'s 2019 [island formula](../concepts/island-formula.md) result showing closed universes have one-dimensional [Hilbert spaces](../concepts/hilbert-space.md).

## Current Approaches

### Observer-Dependent Boundaries

[Edgar Shaghoulian](../people/edgar-shaghoulian.md) (UC Santa Cruz) proposed that introducing an observer into a closed universe restores its informational complexity, analogous to how subdivisions enlarge Hilbert spaces in topological field theories. [Ying Zhao](../people/ying-zhao.md), [Daniel Harlow](../people/daniel-harlow.md), and Usatyuk (MIT, 2025) formalized this: the observer introduces a boundary around itself, expanding the Hilbert space. If correct, this implies observer-independent descriptions of closed universes may be impossible — a fundamental shift from physics' traditional pursuit of an objective "view from nowhere."

### Black Hole Warm-Up

Physicists use black hole [holography](../concepts/holography.md) as a stepping stone, since black holes share the horizon structure of de Sitter space. Hartman: "Anytime we make progress on black holes, we go back and ask: Can we apply this to de Sitter?" Progress has been significant on the black hole side, but translation to de Sitter remains elusive.

### Separating Technical from Conceptual Problems

Green advocates systematically identifying which problems are technically solvable versus conceptually novel, to avoid "confusing them with the smaller, more tractable problems."

### Challenging Flat-Space Intuition

Penedones argues that studying de Sitter space is valuable even for its own sake: "De Sitter tells you that your intuition that you develop in flat space is not true in all spaces."

## Status

The problem remains wide open, but researchers express growing optimism. Hartman notes that the belief in holography extending to de Sitter has "become more convincing" in recent years. [Manuel Loparco](../people/manuel-loparco.md) sees the field as ripe: "It's really low-hanging fruit that we're picking."
