# Supermassive Black Hole Origins

JWST observations have revealed supermassive black holes in the early universe that are too massive to have formed through conventional stellar collapse and merger processes. The discovery of QSO1 — a 50-million-solar-mass "naked" black hole without a galaxy, existing just 750 million years after the Big Bang — has made this problem acute. The object's primordial hydrogen composition suggests it formed before stars had enriched the surrounding region with heavier elements.

[Marta Volonteri](../people/marta-volonteri.md) (Paris Institute of Astrophysics): "The most plausible explanation seems to be [that] the black hole developed before the galaxy."

## The Timing Problem

By the universe's first billion years, observations reveal supermassive black holes anchoring galaxies. Yet the conventional growth process — where stellar-mass black holes merge and accrete over time — appears too slow to produce such massive objects in that timeframe.

## Competing Formation Theories

### Primordial Black Holes

Stephen Hawking's 1971 proposal: the Big Bang itself created black holes from density fluctuations in the early universe. These [primordial black holes](../concepts/primordial-black-holes.md) could have grown over hundreds of millions of years to reach QSO1's size. This connects to the broader PBH research around dark matter candidates and [Hawking radiation](../concepts/hawking-radiation.md) detection.

### Not-Quite-Primordial Black Holes

Wenzer Qin (NYU) proposes that dense regions formed but didn't immediately collapse. Over hundreds of thousands of years, these evolved into gas clouds too massive to become stars, eventually collapsing directly into black holes.

### Runaway Growth

[Priyamvada Natarajan](../people/priyamvada-natarajan.md) (Yale) and Tal Alexander (Weizmann Institute) suggest a scenario where one star in a dense early-universe region collapses into a large black hole that rapidly consumes surrounding gas and stars, then persists alone after the other stars exhaust themselves.

### Direct Collapse

Gas clouds in the early universe, prevented from cooling and fragmenting by residual Big Bang radiation, may have collapsed directly into black holes without the intermediate stellar stage. This bypasses the mass bottleneck of stellar collapse.

## Current Status

QSO1 and the 340+ [Little Red Dots](../concepts/little-red-dots.md) identified by the [James Webb Space Telescope](../experiments/james-webb-space-telescope.md) provide the observational evidence, but no formation model has achieved consensus. As John Regan (Maynooth University) puts it, the situation resembles developing theories about human development by studying only adults and teenagers, then discovering toddlers — "messy new entities that are hard for researchers to interpret based on what they've seen before."

[Jenny Greene](../people/jenny-greene.md) (Princeton): "Every time we think we understand something [about LRDs], they surprise us again by not having the properties we expect."

The LRD puzzle adds a specific new challenge: if these objects are AGN, their black hole masses are 10–50% of their host galaxy masses — extreme compared to the local 0.1% ratio. This would imply black hole growth dominated over star formation in the early universe, reversing the sequence seen today.
