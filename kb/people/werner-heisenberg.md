# Werner Heisenberg

Werner Heisenberg (1901–1976) was a German theoretical physicist who founded quantum mechanics in 1925 during an isolated stay on the North Sea island of Helgoland. He received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1932.

## The Helgoland Breakthrough

Suffering from hay fever, Heisenberg retreated to Helgoland in June 1925 to escape pollen. There, he developed a radical reorientation of atomic physics: rather than trying to visualize atomic structures directly — an impossible task given the experimental tools available — he restricted his theory entirely to measurable quantities: the color and intensity of light emitted by atoms.

This observation-based framework led to the mathematical arrays (matrices) that form quantum mechanics' algebraic foundation. Together with Max Born and Pascual Jordan, he developed this into full matrix mechanics — the first complete formulation of quantum mechanics.

## Uncertainty Principle

In 1927, Heisenberg derived the uncertainty principle: the position and momentum of a particle cannot both be precisely known simultaneously. The more precisely one is measured, the less precisely the other can be determined. This is not a limitation of measurement instruments but a fundamental feature of quantum reality.

## Copenhagen Interpretation and the "Cut"

Heisenberg, along with Niels Bohr, developed the [Copenhagen interpretation](../open-questions/measurement-problem.md) of quantum mechanics. A central feature is "Heisenberg's cut": a conceptual boundary dividing the quantum domain (where the [wave function](../concepts/wave-function.md) applies) from the classical domain (where measuring devices and observers operate). Below the cut, quantum superpositions reign; above it, classical physics applies.

Modern experiments showing quantum behavior in macroscopic objects have blurred any sharp version of this cut, and decoherence theory offers a mechanism for the transition that doesn't require one.

## Legacy

The 2025 Helgoland centennial conference — attended by approximately 300 physicists — marked 100 years since Heisenberg's breakthrough. Despite quantum mechanics' spectacular technological success, no consensus on its interpretation has emerged. As one attendee noted: "We are here happily celebrating 100 years, but actually it's a mess. We were given this theory, and we still don't understand what it means."
