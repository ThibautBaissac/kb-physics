# Vlatko Vedral

Vlatko Vedral is a physicist at the University of Oxford working on quantum foundations and quantum information.

## Axioms of Quantum Mechanics

Vedral is a prominent voice in the debate over the mathematical foundations of quantum mechanics. In the context of 2025 real-valued reformulations of quantum theory, he observed that even without explicit use of the imaginary number *i*, the underlying arithmetic remains inescapably complex: "You can write them down whichever way you like, but it's unavoidable that they have to multiply exactly as though they were [complex numbers](../concepts/complex-numbers-in-quantum-mechanics.md)."

Rather than viewing the real-vs-complex debate as settled, Vedral advocates for finding simpler axioms for quantum mechanics — intuitive principles from which the theory could be re-derived in an entirely new form. He frames this as a deeper open question: "We really don't have a single alternative to how quantum mechanics was already done 100 years ago. And the question is, why? Why can't we go beyond this?"

## Thermodynamic Entanglement Witnesses

In 2004, Vedral and Časlav Brukner (University of Vienna) proposed that macroscopic thermodynamic measurements — such as heat capacity or magnetic field response — can serve as "witnesses" revealing [quantum entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) between particles. This idea was later developed into a practical scheme by [Patryk Lipka-Bartosik](../people/patryk-lipka-bartosik.md) and then into a non-destructive "thermometer for quantumness" by [Alexssandre de Oliveira Jr.](../people/alexssandre-de-oliveira.md) and colleagues (2025).

Vedral endorses the scheme's advantages of simplicity and generality for verifying quantum computer performance: "These verification protocols are very important." He notes that implementing the idea requires very good system control and that the method won't detect all entangled states. On the prospect of using thermodynamic measurements to test whether gravity is quantum: "Wouldn't it be lovely if you could do something as easy and macroscopic as this?"
