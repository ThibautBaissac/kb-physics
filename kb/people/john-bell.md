# John Bell

John Stewart Bell (1928–1990) was an Irish physicist at CERN who derived **Bell's theorem** in 1964 — a mathematical result showing that no theory of local hidden variables can reproduce all the predictions of quantum mechanics.

## Bell's Theorem

Einstein's 1935 EPR argument proposed that quantum mechanics was incomplete: entangled particles must carry hidden variables that predetermine their measurement outcomes, preserving local realism. Bell devised a testable inequality: if local hidden variables exist, then the correlations between measurements on entangled particles must satisfy a certain mathematical bound.

Quantum mechanics predicts violations of this bound. Experiments — beginning with Clauser, Holt, Shimony, and Holt (1969), and definitively by Aspect (1981) and later loophole-free experiments — confirmed quantum mechanics' predictions. Local hidden-variable theories are experimentally ruled out.

See [Bell's Theorem](../concepts/bells-theorem.md) for the full conceptual account.

## Implications for Reality

Bell's result forces a stark choice: either (1) quantum systems lack definite properties before measurement (contra local realism), or (2) measurements on separated particles influence each other instantaneously (nonlocality, in tension with relativity). No local realistic picture of [quantum entanglement](../concepts/quantum-entanglement.md) is possible.

This result is central to the [measurement problem](../open-questions/measurement-problem.md): any interpretation of quantum mechanics must explain or accommodate Bell's nonlocality.
